A Hawkish Fed and a Peace Deal Landed in the Same Week. Wall Street Bought the Peace.

Stocks rallied into a long weekend even as Kevin Warsh's first Fed meeting put a 2026 rate hike on the table — because the US–Iran deal is doing the disinflation the Fed says it still fears. US markets are shut today for Juneteenth.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The Fed turned hawkish; stocks rose anyway. The new dot plot pencils in a possible 2026 hike, yet the Nasdaq still closed up 1.91% as chips and small caps led the bid.
  2. The US–Iran deal is signed. A 14-point memorandum reopens the Strait of Hormuz; CENTCOM lifted its naval blockade and the first supertankers have transited.
  3. Oil keeps sliding. Brent fell to $78.34 — down roughly 14% in five sessions — and US pump prices slipped below $4 for the first time since March.
  4. Intel–Apple lit up semis. Trump said Apple will design and build chips with Intel in the US; Intel jumped ~11% and dragged the chip complex higher.
  5. Wall Street is dark today. US cash markets are closed for Juneteenth, leaving Europe's open and UK retail sales as the only liquidity events before the weekend.

The Big Picture

The week's defining tension was settled, for now, in Thursday's tape: a Federal Reserve that just told markets it may raise rates this year, versus a geopolitical de-escalation that is quietly pulling the inflation rug out from under that very threat. Under new Chair Kevin Warsh, the FOMC held the funds rate at 3.50–3.75% but lifted its 2026 median dot to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, scrubbing the prior cut and signalling at least one hike — while marking growth down and inflation up. On paper, that is a mildly stagflationary forecast and an unambiguously hawkish message. Equities should have flinched. Instead the Nasdaq rose nearly 2% and the Russell 2000 led with a 2.1% gain.

The reconciliation sits in the commodity pits. The signed US–Iran framework and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz have knocked Brent down about 14% in a week and pushed US gasoline back below $4 a gallon — unwinding the energy shock that drove headline CPI toward 3.3% in the first place. Markets are, in effect, betting that peace does the Fed's tightening for it: if crude keeps falling, the inflation impulse Warsh is guarding against fades faster than the dot plot assumes, and the threatened hike never arrives. The bond market is voting the same way — the 10-year slipped to 4.46% despite the hawkish projections, and rate-sensitive small caps led the rally. That is not a market that believes the Fed's own forecast.

Markets

Equities: breadth, not just the megacaps. 293 of the S&P 500's members advanced on Thursday, and 14 of its 20 top performers were technology names. Intel surged about 11% after Trump said Apple would design and build chips with it in the US, lifting SanDisk, Super Micro and Marvell in sympathy. The losers told their own story — Accenture cratered ~17%, its worst day ever, as Wall Street sours on consulting and software exposed to AI disruption, and newly public SpaceX fell a second day, slipping into a technical bear market after briefly topping Amazon's value.

Rates & FX: the curve won't price the hike. The 10-year yield eased to 4.46% and the 2-year sat near 4.19%, a curve that refuses to price the hike the Fed just floated. The dollar index, by contrast, pushed to a 14-month high near 100.6 — a hawkish Fed plus a calmer Middle East is a potent combination for the greenback, and a headwind that helped pin gold and bitcoin lower.

Commodities & crypto: the war premium drains. Brent settled at $78.34 and WTI at $75.14, both down sharply as Saudi supertankers resumed Hormuz transits; crude is still up more than 30% from pre-war levels, leaving room to fall further if the truce holds. Gold dropped 2.4% to about $4,275 as its haven bid drained, and bitcoin slid 1.37% to $64,326, pressured by the firmer dollar.

"The 10-year yield fell the same week the Fed threatened to hike. The bond market has decided cheaper oil matters more than a hawkish dot plot."

Warsh's First Test

Kevin Warsh's debut as chair reshaped the Fed's signal more than its setting. Rates were held, but the Summary of Economic Projections delivered the hawkish jolt: nine of eighteen participants now see at least one hike by year-end, eight see no change, and just one expects a cut. True to his long-standing distaste for forward guidance, Warsh withheld his own dot — the projection carries 18 submissions, not the usual 19 — leaving the median to do the talking at 3.8%. The market's challenge is obvious: the committee is bracing for inflation that the collapse in energy prices may already be defusing.

The Bottom Line

This was the week the Fed warned of a hike and the market shrugged — because a signed peace deal and a 14% drop in crude are unwinding the inflation that gave the warning its teeth. The path of risk assets from here runs through the Strait of Hormuz, not the dot plot: hold the truce and cheaper oil neutralises Warsh's hawkish tilt, letting equities grind higher; break it, and both the war premium and the rate threat come roaring back at once. For now, traders are pricing peace. Today's holiday-thinned tape is no place to test that conviction.
