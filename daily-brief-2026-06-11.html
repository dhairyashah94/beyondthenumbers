A 4.2% CPI Meets a Two-Front War — and the Fed's Next Move May Be a Hike

Overnight on Wall Street, the hottest inflation print in three years collided with a second day of US strikes on Iran. The Dow lost 953 points, Brent holds near $96, and futures markets are now pricing a rate increase — not a cut — as the Fed's most likely next move.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Inflation broke 4%. May CPI rose 4.2% year on year, the highest since April 2023, with energy up 23.5% and responsible for over 60% of the monthly gain — though core rose just 0.2% on the month.
  2. The conflict widened. The US struck Iranian targets for a second straight day, Iran hit Kuwait International Airport and US facilities in Bahrain and Jordan, and Washington says Tehran has mined "large segments" of the Strait of Hormuz.
  3. Wall Street sold off hard. The Dow fell 953 points (1.87%), the S&P 500 lost 1.62% to 7,266.99, and the VIX jumped almost 12% to 22.22.
  4. Oracle beat and still fell. Record Q4 revenue of $19.2 billion and a $638 billion backlog couldn't offset a 162% jump in capex to $55.7 billion; shares dropped more than 7% after hours on plans to raise roughly $20 billion in new capital.
  5. The Fed pivot has inverted. Futures put a ~97% probability on a hold at next week's meeting — and roughly 70% odds on a quarter-point hike by December, up from about 45% in late May.

The Big Picture

For six months the market's base case was that the Fed's next move would be a cut. Wednesday's CPI print ended that argument. Headline inflation at 4.2% — a third consecutive monthly acceleration, driven by an energy complex up 23.5% year on year — is not a number a central bank eases into, even one staring at a federal funds range of 3.50%–3.75% that it considered restrictive a year ago. The telling detail is what didn't move: core CPI rose just 0.2% on the month, undershooting forecasts, with the annual core rate at 2.9%. The economy is not overheating; it is being taxed by oil. That distinction is why the 10-year yield, after touching 4.55%, actually eased to 4.53% by the close — the bond market sees an energy levy on growth, not a wage-price spiral.

Equities couldn't take the same comfort, because the policy implication cuts one way. An energy shock the Fed cannot fix, layered on a conflict with no visible off-ramp — Iran mining the Strait of Hormuz, the US restricting Iranian ports, strikes now reaching Kuwait and Bahrain — leaves the Fed pinned and earnings exposed. What the market is pricing is not recession; it is the removal of the policy safety net. Goldman Sachs has already pulled its forecast for any 2026 cut, and futures now lean toward a December hike. With the FOMC entering its June 16–17 meeting, every data point between now and Wednesday — starting with this afternoon's PPI — is being read as a test of whether 4.2% is a peak or a staging post.

Markets

Equities. The selloff was broad and accelerated into the close, with all three major US indexes finishing near session lows; the Nasdaq's 1.98% drop extended a brutal stretch for tech that included last week's 4% single-day rout in chip stocks. Europe escaped lightly — the Stoxx 600 slipped just 0.08% to 618.17, having closed before the full New York unwind. This morning in Asia the pressure carried over: Tokyo's Nikkei fell around 2% in morning trade, and Hong Kong's Hang Seng — already near its lowest level since late March after five straight losing sessions — stayed under pressure alongside mainland benchmarks.

Rates & FX. The 10-year Treasury ended at 4.53%, off its 4.55% intraday high — a muted move that says the bond market is treating this as a relative-price shock, not an inflation regime change. The policy-sensitive front end is doing the repricing instead: a hold next week is near-certain, and December hike odds have roughly leapt from 45% to 70% in two weeks. The dollar index reclaimed 100.06 on safe-haven flows, with the euro pinned near $1.15.

Commodities & Crypto. Brent crude surged above $96 on Wednesday — its second day of gains — before easing about 1.5% to $96.38 in Asian trading this morning; WTI trades near $94.79. US crude inventories fell 7.2 million barrels, a seventh straight weekly draw. Gold's slide toward $4,110 leaves it roughly 25% below January's record — undercut by the rising yields its own inflation story produces. Bitcoin sits at $61,996, barely moved overnight and still nursing this month's break below $60,000.

"The market spent six months pricing the Fed's next move as a cut. It now prices a hike — and it was oil, not the labor market, that made the call."

The Fed's Inverted Pivot

The repricing in rate markets is the cleanest expression of what changed this month. Two weeks ago a December hike was a coin flip; a strong jobs report pushed it to 70%, and Wednesday's CPI cemented it. For next week's meeting, futures assign roughly 96–98% probability to no change in the 3.50%–3.75% range — but the statement and projections will be scrutinized for how the committee weighs a 4.2% headline rate against a 2.9% core. The Fed's dilemma is acute: tightening into an oil shock punishes growth without producing barrels, yet doing nothing while headline inflation accelerates for a third straight month risks unanchoring expectations. Chair Powell's path of least regret is hawkish patience — hold, talk tough, and let the December meeting carry the optionality. That is exactly what the curve now shows.

Earnings: Oracle's $55.7 Billion Problem

Overnight, after the US close, Oracle delivered what should have been a triumphant print: Q4 revenue of $19.18 billion (up 21%, ahead of the $19.10 billion consensus), adjusted EPS of $2.03 versus $1.96 expected, total cloud revenue up 47% to $9.9 billion, and OCI infrastructure revenue up 93% to $5.8 billion. Remaining performance obligations jumped $85 billion in a single quarter to $638 billion — almost entirely large-scale AI contracts. The stock still fell more than 7% in extended trading.

The reason sits on the cash flow statement, not the income statement. Capital expenditure surged 162% to $55.7 billion for the fiscal year, and management plans to raise roughly $20 billion in fresh capital to keep building. With the 10-year at 4.53% and the Fed leaning hawkish, the market is no longer paying for backlog growth funded by borrowed money — it wants evidence that a $638 billion order book converts to margin before the financing bill arrives. Watch the AI-capex complex at the 5:30 PM GST open: if Oracle's pattern of beat-and-fall spreads to the rest of the buildout trade, that says more about the rate environment than about AI demand.

The Bottom Line

The soft-landing trade assumed the Fed would always be there to catch falling markets. Wednesday repriced that assumption: with headline inflation at 4.2% and a war pressing on the world's most important oil chokepoint, the safety net is gone — and markets must now stand on earnings alone, at $96 oil. Until either Tehran or the CPI blinks, every rally is renting conviction it hasn't paid for.
