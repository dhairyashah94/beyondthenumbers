Five Things to Know Before the Open

  1. The ceasefire is over. US Central Command began what it called self-defense strikes on Iran at 5pm ET Tuesday after an Apache helicopter was downed over the Strait of Hormuz. Futures slid on the news.
  2. CPI lands at 8:30am ET. Consensus sees May headline inflation at +0.5% on the month and 4.2% on the year — the hottest since 2023 — with core at 2.9%. Energy is doing the damage: up 23.5% year-on-year.
  3. Rate cuts have left the building. Traders have priced out Fed easing through end-2027 and now assign roughly one-in-three odds of a hike by December. The FOMC meets June 16–17.
  4. Tech is bleeding leadership. The S&P 500 tech sector fell nearly 2% Tuesday; capital keeps rotating into cheaper markets abroad. The Nikkei sits at records, up over 30% year-to-date.
  5. Oracle reports tonight — the week's cleanest read on whether AI infrastructure demand justifies the spend. Chewy reports this morning.

The Big Picture: A 17-Day Ceasefire, Priced for Permanence

Markets had been trading the US–Iran truce as a done deal — oil drifted lower for most of last week, gold gave back its war premium, and equities pushed toward records on rotation rather than fear. Tuesday's helicopter incident over the Strait of Hormuz exposed how thin that conviction was. Within an hour of the first headlines, the S&P 500 went from flat to sharply lower before clawing back into the close, finishing down 0.26% at 7,386.65. The Nasdaq, heavy with the year's most crowded positioning, fell almost 1%.

The instructive detail is what didn't happen. Brent fell about 3% earlier Tuesday on peace optimism, then held around $91 a barrel as strikes resumed — well below April's $107 panic high. The oil market, in other words, is treating this as a flare-up inside a de-escalation, not a new war. That judgment, more than anything the Fed says this week, is what keeps the inflation outlook from getting materially worse. Watch tanker rates and Hormuz transit headlines: they will tell you before the indices do whether that judgment holds.

Markets: The Rotation Survives the Headlines

Equities. Beneath the red close, breadth tells a different story — advancing issues have been outnumbering decliners even as the headline indexes slip, the signature of money leaving mega-cap tech rather than leaving the market. That rotation has a passport: Tokyo keeps printing records while Hong Kong, down on the year, absorbed another 1.6% drop. The gap between the Nikkei's 30%-plus year-to-date run and the Hang Seng's decline is now one of the widest regional divergences in years.

Rates and FX. The 10-year Treasury yield eased to 4.54%, off near three-week highs — a modest haven bid, nothing more. The dollar index slipped to 99.80, notable mainly for what it refused to do: rally on a war headline. A dollar that won't catch a geopolitical bid suggests the de-dollarization trade of 2026 still has sponsors.

Commodities and crypto. Gold's 3.4% slide to roughly $4,115 — its lowest since late November — is the day's most telling move. Two months ago, any Iran headline added $100 to the gold price. Now, with inflation data matching forecasts and the conflict viewed as contained, the war premium is being methodically unwound. Bitcoin's slip below $62,000 confirms it: crypto is trading as a risk asset, not a refuge.

"When gold falls 3% on the day a ceasefire collapses, the market is telling you which story it actually believes."

The Fed's Box: 4.2% Headline, 2.9% Core, Zero Room

Today's May CPI is expected to show the sharpest headline print since 2023 — 4.2% year-on-year, driven almost entirely by energy, which is running 23.5% above last year. Core, at an expected 2.9%, tells a calmer story. That split is the whole policy debate in miniature: the Fed can look through an oil-shock headline, but only if expectations stay anchored — and futures markets are already flinching, pricing no cuts through 2027 and roughly one-in-three odds of a hike this year.

For CFOs, the practical translation: the era of penciling in cheaper money next year is over. Funding costs at current levels are the base case through 2027, and any refinancing math built on Fed relief deserves a rewrite. The June 16–17 FOMC meeting now carries live risk of a hawkish dot-plot surprise.

The Bottom Line

Three weeks ago this market feared a war. Today it fears a Fed that can't cut. Tuesday's session — stocks resilient, gold crushed, cut odds at zero — shows the second fear has fully displaced the first. Until core inflation breaks below 2.9% or oil breaks below $80, every dip is a rates story wearing a geopolitics costume. Trade the Fed, hedge the war — not the other way around.
