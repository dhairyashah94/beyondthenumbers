The War Premium Drains Out of Crude — and Lifts Everything Else

A US–Iran deal moved within reach on the eve of the G7, knocking nearly 4% off oil and igniting a global risk rally from Tokyo to Frankfurt. The catch: a 4.2% inflation print leaves the Fed cornered before Wednesday.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The deal is the story. President Trump signalled a US–Iran agreement could be signed this weekend on the sidelines of the G7 in France, raising the prospect of a reopened Strait of Hormuz.
  2. Oil paid for the peace. Brent slid 3.78% to $86.98 and WTI fell 3.85% to $84.33 as the geopolitical risk premium that built through early June unwound in hours.
  3. Risk roared back. Tokyo's Nikkei 225 jumped 2.81% to roughly 66,020 and Europe's Stoxx 600 added 1.9%, with Wall Street closing broadly higher into the weekend.
  4. SpaceX arrived. Elon Musk's SpaceX surged about 19% in its trading debut, the year's marquee listing, helping sentiment even as megacap tech lagged the small-cap rally.
  5. The Fed looms. Markets price a 97% chance the Fed holds at 3.50–3.75% on Wednesday, with May CPI at a two-year-high 4.2% keeping cuts off the table.

The Big Picture

For two weeks crude carried a fear tax. Every threat of fresh strikes on Iran, every hint that the Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint for roughly a fifth of seaborne oil — might close, added dollars to the barrel and a discount to risk assets. On Friday that trade ran in reverse. Trump's suggestion that an agreement could be inked at the G7 sent Brent down nearly 4% and pulled the safe-haven bid out of gold, which slipped back below $4,200. What markets are pricing is not peace itself but the removal of a tail: the scenario in which Hormuz shuts, oil spikes toward triple digits, and a fresh energy shock reignites inflation just as the Fed hoped to be done with it. Strip that risk out and the whole board re-rates — equities up, the dollar's haven premium down, volatility lower.

The tell is in the leadership. The session's winners were the war's losers: travel, construction and autos in Europe, cyclicals and small caps in the US, where the Russell 2000 outpaced a lagging Nasdaq. That is the signature of a relief rally driven by falling input costs and receding macro risk, not by a new wave of AI enthusiasm. The hard part comes next. A deal cuts the energy-price threat to inflation, but it does nothing for the 4.2% annual CPI already on the books — and a calmer oil market removes one of the few arguments the Fed had for sounding dovish. Friday rewarded the de-escalation; Wednesday will test whether disinflation can survive without it.

Markets

Equities: cyclicals lead, megacap lags. Wall Street closed the week broadly higher: the Dow added 0.70% to 51,202.26, the S&P 500 rose 0.50% to 7,431.46 and the Nasdaq Composite lagged with a 0.31% gain to 25,888.84 as money rotated out of crowded megacap tech toward rate- and oil-sensitive cyclicals. SpaceX's roughly 19% debut pop set the tone for risk appetite. Overnight in Asia the rally was sharper — the Nikkei 225 leapt 2.81% to about 66,020 — while Europe's Stoxx 600 climbed 1.9%, led by travel and autos. Hong Kong was the outlier, with the Hang Seng slipping 0.7% for a seventh straight loss to its lowest since July 2025.

Rates & FX: the bond market shrugs. Treasuries were quiet against the drama in commodities: the 10-year yield held near 4.48%, up a couple of basis points on the day but down roughly 10 from mid-week as the inflation threat from oil receded. The dollar index sat at 99.80, essentially flat, having shed its haven bid earlier in the week; EUR/USD hovered near 1.165. The muted bond reaction is itself a signal — markets read a deal as disinflationary at the margin, not as a reason to reprice the Fed.

Commodities & crypto: crude gives back the premium. Crude was the epicentre: Brent fell 3.78% to $86.98 and WTI dropped 3.85% to $84.33, both back to mid-April levels as traders priced in a reopened Hormuz. Gold eased 0.1% to about $4,195 an ounce as safe-haven demand cooled, though it held most of the week's gains. Bitcoin bucked the haven unwind, rising 1.65% to $63,566 as broad risk appetite lifted the complex.

"A peace deal removes the energy shock the Fed feared — but it cannot remove the 4.2% inflation already in the data. Friday was the easy half of the story."

The Fed's Narrow Path

Wednesday's FOMC decision arrives with the central bank boxed into a corner of its own making. May CPI ran at 4.2% year-on-year — the hottest since April 2023 — and 0.5% on the month, propelled by a 3.9% surge in energy. With unemployment steady at 4.3% and payrolls holding, futures put the odds of a hold at the 3.50–3.75% target range at roughly 97%, per CME FedWatch. The irony of Friday's oil drop is that it solves tomorrow's inflation problem while doing nothing for today's: the energy spike that would have justified a hawkish hold is fading, yet the realised inflation that ties the Fed's hands is already printed.

The subplot is communication. June is a Summary of Economic Projections meeting, normally the quarter's marquee event for the dot plot. But reporting suggests the Fed may begin retiring forward guidance as soon as this meeting — potentially dropping the rate-path forecast and stripping directional bias from the statement. For a market that has leaned on the dots for years, less guidance means wider rate uncertainty and, plausibly, a higher term premium. Chair Powell's tone at 10:30 PM Dubai time will matter more than the unchanged rate itself.

The Bottom Line

The market just bought the easy half of the trade: a falling oil price and a fading war premium are a clean tailwind for risk. The hard half lands Wednesday, when a Fed staring at 4.2% inflation has to decide how much guidance to give a market that has grown dependent on it. Peace in the Gulf removes tomorrow's inflation threat; it does nothing for today's. Enjoy the relief rally — but watch the dots.
