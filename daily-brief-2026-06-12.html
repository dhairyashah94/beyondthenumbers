Trump Calls Off the Iran Strikes — and the Peace Trade Detonates

A sweeping Wall Street rally met the largest IPO in history overnight: oil sank 3.6%, the Dow reclaimed 50,000, and SpaceX priced a $75 billion offering hours before its Nasdaq debut today. Asia is extending the rally this morning.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The Iran strikes are off. President Trump cancelled Thursday's planned attack, saying a settlement has been "approved by all parties" at the highest level of Iranian leadership and could be signed in Europe within days.
  2. SpaceX priced the largest IPO in history. The company sold 555.6 million shares at $135 apiece — a $75 billion raise at roughly a $1.77 trillion valuation — and begins trading on Nasdaq today under SPCX.
  3. US wholesale inflation is on fire. May producer prices jumped 1.1% on the month against a 0.7% consensus, pushing the annual rate to 6.5% — the highest since November 2022 — on a 23.4% surge in gasoline.
  4. The ECB hiked for the first time since 2023, lifting its deposit rate 25 basis points to 2.25% and raising its 2026 inflation forecast to 3.0%, with Lagarde calling the move "robust across three scenarios."
  5. Asia is surging this morning. The Nikkei is up 3.8% at 66,640, Seoul's Kospi has jumped more than 7%, and Hong Kong and mainland China are 1.3–1.6% higher as the war-risk discount unwinds.

The Big Picture

Strip away the relief and look at what actually repriced overnight. Brent fell $3.37 to $89.73 — still elevated, but no longer carrying a Strait-of-Hormuz premium. The VIX collapsed 12.5% to 19.44, back below the 20 line that has separated war regime from normal trading since March. The 10-year Treasury yield eased 8 basis points to 4.46%. That combination tells you the market is not pricing peace as a growth story; it is pricing peace as an inflation story. Hours before Trump's announcement, May PPI printed its hottest monthly gain since the series began tracking goods this way, and futures had begun flirting with the unthinkable — a Fed hike. A signed deal that takes energy out of the inflation pipeline removes that tail risk, which is why equities and gold rallied together: gold's 2.5% rebound from a seven-month low of $4,080 was a bet on fewer hikes, not more fear.

The risk is sequencing. The deal is announced, not signed; the inflation is printed, not reversed. May CPI is already running 4.2% year-on-year and PPI at 6.5%, and those numbers will not bend for at least two more readings even if Brent keeps falling. The Fed meets Tuesday and Wednesday with futures assigning roughly 97% odds to a hold — but holding at 3.50–3.75% with wholesale inflation at a three-and-a-half-year high is an uncomfortable place to stand. Markets spent Thursday buying the end of the war. The harder question, which lands at next week's FOMC, is whether the war's inflation outlives it.

Markets

Equities: chips led the charge. The Nasdaq's 2.54% gain — a sharp reversal from the early-June semiconductor rout — was powered by Lam Research (+12.7%), Micron (+11%), Intel (+10%) and AMD (+8%), with positioning ahead of today's SpaceX debut adding fuel. Europe closed before the strike cancellation broke: the Stoxx 600 still rose 0.6% on the chip rebound, with ASML up 4.5%. This morning Tokyo's Nikkei is up 3.8% at 66,640 and the Kospi has surged more than 7%. In the Gulf, the deal matters most for Dubai — the DFM has lost roughly 16% since March while Tadawul rode high oil to a 5.8% gain; a signed settlement would start closing that gap when UAE markets trade today and Tadawul reopens Sunday.

Rates & FX: the hike tail gets clipped. The 10-year yield fell 8 basis points to 4.46% as the peace headlines outweighed a brutal PPI print. Futures price roughly 97% odds the Fed holds at 3.50–3.75% next Wednesday, though markets still flag meaningful odds of a hike by October if energy disinflation stalls. The dollar index slipped below 100 to 99.86; the euro was flat — remarkably, a first ECB hike in nearly three years couldn't move it, a sign currency markets see the eurozone's 25 basis points as defensive, not hawkish.

Commodities & crypto: crude gives back the war premium. Brent settled down $3.37 at $89.73 and slipped further toward $89.30 in Asian hours; WTI closed at $86.83. Gold rebounded from $4,080 — its lowest since November — to about $4,218, and is holding near $4,200 this morning. Bitcoin added 1.3% to $63,429, participating in the risk rally but hardly leading it.

"Oil at $89 doesn't end the inflation fight — it ends the argument for a rate hike. That distinction is worth every point of Thursday's rally."

The Inflation Already in the Pipeline

The deal arrived a few hours too late for the price data. May PPI rose 1.1% on the month — gasoline alone jumped 23.4%, final-demand goods posted their largest increase on record, and stage-one intermediate demand surged 3.2%, the steepest since the series began in 2009. Annual wholesale inflation now runs at 6.5%, fully 2.3 points above CPI's 4.2%, which is itself the hottest since April 2023. That gap is the problem: producer prices lead consumer prices, meaning the war's energy shock is still working its way toward households even as the war itself winds down. The ECB read the same data and moved first, hiking to 2.25% while cutting its 2026 growth forecast to 0.8% — a stagflation trade-off Lagarde defended bluntly: if inflation runs out of control, "it becomes much more difficult to bring it back."

The Fed's position next week is more delicate. Holding at 3.50–3.75% is near-certain — futures put it around 97% — but the committee must decide how to talk about a shock that may already be reversing. Cut the inflation forecast too eagerly and a collapsed deal embarrasses them; validate the hike chatter and they tighten financial conditions into a fragile détente. Watch the dots and the press conference, not the decision.

The Bottom Line

Thursday's rally was the market closing its short position on peace — but peace, if it sticks, only solves the next inflation report, not the last three. With PPI at 6.5%, the ECB already hiking and the Fed deciding next week, the trade from here is not risk-on versus risk-off; it is whether disinflation arrives faster than the pipeline pressure already in motion. Watch oil, not the ceremony: Brent below $85 validates everything bought yesterday. Brent back above $95 takes it all away.
