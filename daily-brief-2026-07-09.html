Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Ceasefire over. Trump declared the Iran deal "off the table" after the US launched strikes; Tehran counterattacked on tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, raising energy supply risk across the Gulf.
  2. Oil spikes on geopolitics. Brent jumped 5% to $78/bbl and WTI rallied 5% as the US revoked Iran's crude-sales waiver, dragging equities lower despite tech's relative resilience.
  3. Inflation anxiety returns. The 10-year yield shot up to 4.57% as traders repriced rate expectations; CME FedWatch now shows ~70% odds of a September hike, up from 58% yesterday.
  4. Earnings season opens. PepsiCo reports Q2 results (5:30 PM GST) with consensus EPS of $2.21; analysts have cut targets ahead of the release, questioning pricing power amid oil-driven input-cost fears.
  5. US data slate is thick. Construction Spending, ISM Manufacturing Prices and ISM Manufacturing all land at 5:00 PM GST; each could move rate expectations further if inflation surprises high.

The Big Picture

Geopolitical shock is overriding earnings resilience. Overnight on Wall Street the S&P 500 fell 0.52% and the Dow dropped 1.09% — a gap that reflects bond-and-cyclical damage — while the Nasdaq clung to a +0.20% gain thanks to mega-cap tech's defensive appeal. The real story is oil. When Trump declared the Iran ceasefire "over" and the US Treasury revoked Iran's ability to sell crude, Brent jumped to $78 from $74.25 the prior close, and WTI rallied past $75. That five-point move in crude, combined with talk of potential US blockades of Iranian oil, has reset the investment playbook: renewed inflation risk is now colliding with an unchanged Fed funds rate and rising expectations for tighter policy ahead. The dollar index held near 101 — a sign haven flows are less panicked than in a pure risk-off episode — and gold fell 0.45% to $4,087, a tell that energy-supply fears are outweighing pure safe-haven demand.

What matters most over the next 24 hours is whether this energy spike is transient geopolitical noise or the opening of a sustained inflation repricing. PepsiCo's earnings before the open and three major US data releases this evening — Construction Spending, ISM Manufacturing Prices and ISM Manufacturing at 5:00 PM GST — will test whether underlying inflation is truly sticky or whether a cooling labor market has done enough. If this morning's Asian session shows Shanghai and Hong Kong holding losses while Tokyo rebounds on a weak yen, expect the bar for a September Fed hike to rise further and rate-sensitive tech to face more pressure into the close.

Markets

Equities. US indices closed mixed. The S&P 500 fell to 7,465 (−0.52%), the Nasdaq Composite held at 25,871 (+0.20%), and the Dow dropped 576 points to 52,348 (−1.09%). Overnight in Asia, Tokyo's Nikkei opened down 0.86% as traders rotated out of mega-cap tech amid rising rate expectations; Hong Kong's Hang Seng futures implied a flat-to-slightly-higher open near 23,061 ahead of PepsiCo and the US data. The FTSE 100 posted a small gain (+0.30% to 10,684), while the Stoxx Europe 600 fell 0.6% mid-morning as oil volatility rippled through the continent's energy and industrial sectors.

Rates & FX. The 10-year yield jumped to 4.57% on the overnight oil fears. CME FedWatch shows ~70% odds of a September hike, up from 58%, pushing two-year yields higher and flattening the curve further. Fed funds futures are pricing a path toward 3.8% by October and near 4% by year-end, keeping the "higher for longer" narrative intact. The dollar index rose 0.05% to 101.07, stable despite the geopolitical risk-off; EUR/USD held near session lows as ECB speakers grew cautious on rate cuts.

Commodities & crypto. Oil led the session. Brent surged 5% to $78/bbl while WTI rallied 5% into the $74 range after the US revoked Iran's crude-sales waiver and threatened further blockades. Gold fell 0.45% to $4,087, pressured by rising real yields and a firmer dollar. Bitcoin declined 1.81% to about $61,840, tracking risk-off equity flows. Copper held steady, suggesting physical demand for durable goods remains intact despite the inflation anxiety.

The Inflation Redux: Why Today's Oil Spike Matters More Than It Should

The Iran ceasefire collapse is a textbook reminder that energy-supply shocks can bypass monetary policy. Crude at $78 Brent — up 19% from the June low of $65.50 — arrives when core PCE inflation sits at 4.2% year-on-year, well above the Fed's 2% target. A hot June CPI print has already forced Fed speakers to emphasize patience and data-dependency. But oil is impatient. A sustained move to $85+ Brent could push terminal-rate expectations higher and crimp consumer spending faster than the labor market can cool. The Bottom Line

Trump's end to the Iran ceasefire is not merely geopolitical theater — it is a repricing event. Oil is now the variable that matters most to Fed expectations, and every $5 move in Brent maps to roughly 5–10 basis points in hike odds. Inflation may have peaked in a headline sense, but energy-supply risk has reset the conversation. Today's US data and PepsiCo's earnings will test whether the consumer can withstand both higher rates and higher energy costs at once. For now, expect volatility, continued Dow underperformance, and a 4.2% core PCE print to dominate the tape more than any single data surprise. The Fed's July 28–29 meeting is effectively a "hold and assess" event; what matters is whether oil stays above $75 — and whether the labor market cracks before September.
