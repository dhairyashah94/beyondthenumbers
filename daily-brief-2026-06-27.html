The AI Trade Cracks — and This Time There Is No Fed Put to Catch It

An OpenAI IPO delay lit the fuse; a hawkish Warsh Fed means Wall Street can no longer assume rescue. Seoul fell 7.7%, Tokyo 4.4%, and the Nasdaq logged a fifth straight loss.

By Dhairya Shah · 6 min read · Markets are closed for the weekend; data as of Friday's overnight US close and Friday's Asian session, with the new week ahead in view

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The AI unwind has legs. The Nasdaq Composite booked a fifth consecutive losing session on Friday as money rotated out of the megacap-tech and semiconductor complex that has driven the whole market for two years.
  2. OpenAI lit the fuse. A report that the company may delay its IPO to 2027 rather than price below a $1 trillion target forced investors to re-examine every private AI valuation at once — and SoftBank shares cratered more than 12% in Tokyo, erasing roughly $38 billion.
  3. Asia took the brunt. Seoul's KOSPI plunged 7.7% and Tokyo's Nikkei 225 dropped 4.4% as Samsung, SK Hynix and SoftBank led an AI-hardware washout; Hong Kong fell 1.9% and Shanghai 2.1%.
  4. The Fed is leaning hawkish, not dovish. Kevin Warsh's FOMC held at 3.50%–3.75% on June 17 but lifted its inflation track to 3.6%; futures now price a 25bp hike by October — the opposite of a rescue.
  5. Oil and crypto are bleeding for different reasons. Brent slid toward $72, its lowest since February, as the Strait of Hormuz ceasefire reopened tanker traffic; bitcoin sank below $59,000, down nearly 19% in June.

The Big Picture

Friday was not, on the surface, a crash: the S&P 500 closed all but unchanged at 7,354.02. But the index-level calm disguised a violent rotation underneath. What sold off was the AI-and-semiconductor engine — and the trigger was a single report that OpenAI is weighing whether to delay its listing to 2027 rather than accept a valuation beneath Sam Altman's $1 trillion line. Because OpenAI is the reference asset against which the entire private and public AI complex is marked, a wobble in its valuation math propagates instantly: if the most-hyped name in the sector can't clear a trillion in this tape, every 38-times-sales multiple behind it looks suddenly negotiable. The mechanism showed up most brutally in Asia, where SoftBank — the purest listed proxy for AI optimism — shed more than a tenth of its value in a session, and where Seoul's chip-heavy index lost nearly 8%.

What makes this episode different from every dip since 2023 is the policy backdrop. For a decade investors could lean on a reflex: when equities fell hard enough, the Fed eased. That reflex is gone. With headline PCE running near 3.6% and the new Warsh-led committee having just nudged its dot plot toward a hike, the central bank has no room to cushion a valuation reset — and arguably no desire to, given that frothy asset prices are part of what it is trying to cool. The market is therefore pricing two things at once: that AI cash flows may arrive slower than the capex already committed, and that there is no monetary backstop if sentiment turns. That combination — stretched valuations meeting a tightening bias — is why a quiet headline number sits on top of the loudest sector dispersion in months.

Markets

Equities: the damage was in Asia. The S&P 500 finished Friday at 7,354.02 (−0.05%), the Dow at 51,876.11 (−0.09%) and the Nasdaq Composite at 25,297.62 (−0.24%) — its fifth straight decline as semiconductors led the retreat. Europe's Stoxx 600 closed 0.5% lower, dragged by ASML, Infineon and STMicroelectronics. The concentration was in Asia: the KOSPI's 7.7% drop and the Nikkei's 4.4% fall were among the steepest single-session moves of the year, with SoftBank down more than 12%.

Rates & FX: a hawkish hold, not a pivot. The 10-year Treasury yield eased two basis points to 4.38%, down about seven on the week, after a broadly in-line PCE print let traders trim the odds of a near-term hike — though the Warsh Fed's hawkish June dot plot keeps a 2026 move firmly in play. The dollar index slipped 0.19% to 101.25 as the modest risk-off bid favoured the yen and Swiss franc over the greenback.

Commodities & crypto: war premium gone, risk premium back. Brent slid toward $72 and WTI fell 3.5% to about $69.40 — the lowest since late February — as the Strait of Hormuz ceasefire restored tanker flows and stripped the war premium out of crude. Gold held near record territory around $4,050 an ounce, the haven of choice while bonds offer no policy cushion. Bitcoin sank below $59,000, down 2.1% on the day and nearly 19% since the start of June, confirming its role as a high-beta proxy for the same risk appetite now draining out of AI equities.

"For the first time this cycle, the bond market is bracing for a Fed that tightens into a stock-market sell-off — the inverse of the central-bank put investors spent a decade relying on."

The Macro Story: A Hawkish Warsh Fed

The reason this sell-off feels structurally different traces back to June 17, when the Federal Open Market Committee — in its first meeting under Chair Kevin Warsh — held the funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% for a fourth straight time but delivered a distinctly hawkish set of projections. Officials lifted their 2026 headline PCE inflation forecast to 3.6% from 2.7%, and core to 3.3%, pushing the median dot to roughly 3.8% by year-end. Nine of nineteen policymakers now see at least one rate hike this year; six see two. Markets have followed, pricing a 25-basis-point hike by October with no cuts on the horizon through 2027. An economy with above-target, re-accelerating inflation simply does not give a central bank the latitude to ease into falling stocks — and that is the cushion an entire generation of AI-era investors had quietly assumed was still there.

The Bottom Line

The week ended with the market's most crowded trade losing its safety net. AI valuations can survive slower cash flows or a hawkish Fed in isolation — but facing both at once, with no prospect of rate relief if sentiment sours, the burden of proof has flipped from the skeptics to the believers. Watch Thursday's payrolls: a strong number won't just move bond yields, it will tell the AI bulls whether the Fed has any reason at all to come to their rescue. For now, the answer is no.
