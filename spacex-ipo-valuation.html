The SpaceX IPO Math: What a $400 Billion Listing Has to Prove

SpaceX is the most valuable private company on earth and the market has decided it is a buy before it has even priced. We pull the two businesses apart, build the revenue bridge to 2030, and ask what you are actually paying for.

By Dhairya Shah · Equity Research · 18 min read

Our view Constructive, with discipline
Base-case equity value $430 bn
Range (bear–bull) $220 bn – $760 bn
What carries it Starlink, ~80% of value

Every cycle produces one company that the market is desperate to own before it can. In 2026 that company is SpaceX. It does not trade, it does not file, and its most recent valuation — set in a private tender rather than an open market — reportedly put the business north of $400 billion. That number was not produced by a discounted cash-flow model. It was produced by scarcity, by a decade of flawless execution, and by the simple fact that there is no other way to buy exposure to the two fastest-growing franchises in aerospace at once.

So before the roadshow tells you what to think, let us do the unglamorous work: separate the parts, count the cash, and decide what is real. This note values SpaceX the way a sell-side desk would value any pre-IPO franchise — sum-of-the-parts, a revenue bridge to 2030, and three honest scenarios. The figures here are estimates built from public disclosure, reported tender prices and industry data; treat them as a framework, not a fact sheet.

Two companies wearing one logo

The single most important thing to understand about SpaceX is that it is not one business. It is a launch company that incubated a telecom company, and the telecom company is now the larger, faster and more valuable of the two. Any valuation that does not separate them will be wrong, because the market pays radically different multiples for rockets than it does for recurring connectivity revenue.

The launch business — Falcon 9, Falcon Heavy and increasingly Starship — is the cash-generative, competitively dominant core. SpaceX conducts the large majority of the world's orbital launches and is effectively the only provider with a reusable, high-cadence fleet. It is a wonderful business. It is also, by the standards of public markets, a relatively small and lumpy one: launch is a project-revenue business with a finite addressable market measured in the low tens of billions of dollars per year.

Starlink is the opposite. It is a subscription business with millions of users across consumer, enterprise, maritime, aviation and government, growing into a total addressable market — global broadband plus direct-to-cell — measured in the hundreds of billions. It carries the economics the public market actually rewards: recurring revenue, expanding gross margin as the constellation scales, and a widening moat made of spectrum, regulatory licenses and a launch cost no competitor can match because they have to pay SpaceX to reach orbit.

"You are not buying a rocket company that happens to sell internet. You are buying an internet company that happens to own the cheapest road to space."

The revenue bridge

Reported figures and industry estimates put SpaceX group revenue in the mid-teens of billions for 2025, with Starlink already contributing the majority and growing far faster than launch. The shape of the next five years matters more than the level. Our base-case build:

The headline is that a company growing the top line at a ~30% compound rate while turning gross-margin positive at the group level is, structurally, a growth-equity story — not a defense contractor. That is what justifies a premium multiple. It is also what makes the stock fragile: the entire valuation leans on Starlink hitting its subscriber and ARPU ramp, and on Starship moving from cost center to revenue line without blowing up the capex budget.

Note what this says: roughly four-fifths of the value sits in Starlink. The rockets are the brand, the moat and the story, but they are not where most of the money is. An investor buying SpaceX at a $400–450bn valuation is, in substance, paying a premium-software multiple for a satellite-broadband operator and getting the world's best launch company as the collateral. Whether that is a bargain or a trap depends entirely on what you believe about Starlink's terminal margin.

Three scenarios

Single point targets are false precision for a pre-revenue-disclosure name. The honest output is a distribution.

ScenarioKey assumptionEquity value
BullStarlink D2C scales globally; Starship wins heavy-lift & Mars cargo economics$760bn
BaseStarlink ~$44bn rev by 2030 at 60%+ gross margin; launch steady$430bn
BearSubscriber growth plateaus, ARPU compresses, capex stays heavy, competition arrives$220bn

The asymmetry is interesting. The bull case is genuinely enormous because direct-to-cell turns every smartphone on earth into a potential Starlink endpoint, a market no terrestrial carrier can fence off. The bear case is not a wipe-out — even a disappointed SpaceX is a $200bn-plus franchise — but it is a brutal de-rating from the price the private market is currently asking. That is the trade you are being offered: limited downside to the franchise, meaningful downside to the multiple.

"The risk in SpaceX is not that the business fails. It is that you pay 2030's price in 2026 and wait five years to grow into it."

What can break the thesis

Key-man and governance. No name is more entangled with its founder. A dual-class structure is near-certain, which means public shareholders will own the cash flows and almost none of the control. Price the governance discount in, because the market eventually will.

Capital intensity. Both Starship and the next-generation Starlink constellation are enormous, lumpy capex commitments. The story only works if launch cost keeps falling fast enough to keep the constellation's unit economics ahead of replacement cost. That is an engineering bet dressed as a financial one.

Concentration and politics. A large and rising share of revenue touches government — NASA, national security, and increasingly allied defense. That is high-quality, sticky revenue, but it also makes the company a political object in a way a normal telecom is not.

Competition, finally. Amazon's Kuiper, a re-energized field of launch startups, and sovereign constellations from Europe and China are all arriving at once. SpaceX's lead is measured in years, not decades, and the market has a habit of paying for monopolies right up until the moment they stop being one.

The bottom line

SpaceX deserves a premium. It is a generational company with two of the best franchises in the industry and an execution record that humbles every competitor. But "great company" and "great entry price" are different questions, and the IPO will be sold on the first to distract you from the second. At a $400–450bn valuation the base case is roughly fair — you are paid for the franchise and you are betting on Starlink's margin. Below ~$350bn it becomes genuinely attractive. Above ~$550bn you are pre-paying the bull case and underwriting Starship for free.

Our posture into the listing: constructive on the asset, disciplined on the price. Let the lock-ups expire, let the first two earnings reports separate the Starlink narrative from the Starlink numbers, and remember that the best businesses make the worst investments when you buy them at the top of everyone else's enthusiasm.
