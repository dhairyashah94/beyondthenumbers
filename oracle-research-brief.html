Oracle Beat on Every Line — and the Stock Fell 7% Anyway

Oracle closed FY2026 with record revenue, 93% cloud-infrastructure growth and the largest contracted order book in enterprise software history — $638 billion. It also burned $23.7 billion in free cash flow building the data centres meant to deliver it. The market was not disputing the quarter. It was disputing the funding model.

Five Things to Know

  1. Clean beat, every line that counts. Q4 revenue of $19.2bn (+21%) beat the $19.10bn consensus. Adjusted EPS $2.03 vs $1.96 expected. Total cloud revenue +47% to $9.9bn for the quarter.
  2. OCI is now a real hyperscaler. Cloud infrastructure grew 93% to $5.8bn in Q4, 77% for the full year. Oracle is taking AI training and inference workloads — not riding the cloud tide, actively winning share against AWS, Azure and Google.
  3. The backlog number has no precedent. Remaining performance obligations hit $638 billion (+363%), up $85bn in a single quarter. That is roughly 9½ years of FY26 revenue already under contract — the single strongest piece of forward visibility in enterprise software.
  4. The cash equation is the whole debate. $32bn of operating cash flow (record, +54%) minus $55.7bn of CapEx (+162%) equals −$23.7bn of free cash flow — Oracle's first materially negative free-cash-flow year. Management guided net CapEx of ~$70bn in FY27 and ~$40bn of additional financing, including ~$20bn of equity.
  5. The stock fell because of the rate environment, not the quarter. With the 10-year at 4.53% and futures pricing ~70% odds of a December Fed hike, the market is no longer paying for growth funded by borrowed money. Oracle's stock fell 7%+ into a tape that had already repriced the entire AI-capex complex. The financing bill got marked to market.

What the market was actually pricing

There are quarters where a company beats and the stock falls because the market is in a bad mood. This was not one of those. Oracle's after-hours drop was the cleanest expression of a repricing that has been building since May: at 4.53% on the ten-year, with the Fed leaning hawkish and CPI at 4.2%, the cost of capital for a company planning to raise ~$40 billion to fund data centres is measurably higher than it was six months ago. Oracle has chosen — correctly, given the demand — to fund its build-out from the market rather than from free cash flow. That means the Fed sits inside its equity story in a way it simply does not for Microsoft, Meta or Alphabet, all of which finance their equivalent capex from surplus cash. Until free cash flow inflects, every Oracle print will be graded on two report cards at once: the operating story (excellent) and the financing story (still in progress).

The operating story is unambiguously strong. Cloud is now more than half of Oracle's $67.4 billion in annual revenue, overtaking the legacy licence-and-support line for the first time in the company's history. OCI is winning genuine AI workloads on price and on the installed-base moat — the database and ERP lock-in that makes switching brutally expensive. The transformation that, two years ago, almost nobody believed Oracle could execute is, by the numbers, largely done.

"RPO is a promise. CapEx is cash. The entire Oracle debate is the exchange rate between the two."

The backlog: extraordinary, and concentrated

The $638 billion backlog is the single best argument the bulls have — and it deserves two readings. The first: there is no precedent for this number in enterprise software. It rose $85 billion in a single quarter, it represents 9½ years of FY26 revenue already under contract, and it is the most tangible proof that AI infrastructure demand is not a hype cycle. The second: management was candid that the bulk of the increase comes from a small number of very large AI contracts, with roughly $75 billion tied to customer prepayments for GPU capacity or customer-supplied hardware. A $638 billion order book from a diversified enterprise base is a fortress. A $638 billion order book where the marginal dollar comes from a handful of AI labs racing each other to train frontier models is a fortress with a concentrated foundation. RPO is a contract, not cash — it converts on the customer's schedule, at the contract's margin, and only if the counterparty is still standing when the capacity comes online.

The bottom line

This was a genuinely strong print sold into a market that has stopped paying for growth funded by borrowed money. Both halves of that sentence are true simultaneously. Operationally, Oracle posted the best evidence yet that it belongs in the first tier of AI infrastructure. Financially, it is running the most leveraged version of the hyperscaler build-out at the worst point in the rate cycle — and it has told you plainly that the burn continues through FY27.

Our posture: own the backlog, respect the burn. The three numbers that decide the stock from here: (1) the pace of RPO-to-revenue conversion against the $90bn FY27 guide; (2) infrastructure gross margin as utilisation scales; (3) the terms of the ~$20 billion equity raise. First checkpoint: Q1 FY2027 results, September 10. The FOMC next week matters more to this name than to almost any other megacap: Oracle, by choice, has tied its equity story to the price of money.
