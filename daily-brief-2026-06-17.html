Oil's $80 Floor Gives Way as the Truce Holds — and Warsh Walks Into His First Fed Test

Brent has slid to a three-month low overnight, gold is firmer, and Wall Street's record run stalled before tonight's decision — the first of the Kevin Warsh era at the Federal Reserve. A record Dow sits beside a falling Nasdaq, the clearest sign yet that the market is rotating, not retreating.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Oil broke $80. Brent slid more than 5% to a three-month low near $79 as the US–Iran ceasefire held and the war-risk premium kept draining out of the barrel.
  2. The Dow set a record, but breadth thinned. Industrials closed at an all-time high while the Nasdaq fell 1.15% — a rotation out of megacap tech, not a broad advance.
  3. It's Warsh's first decision. The FOMC announces at 10:00 PM GST tonight; futures put a hold at the 3.50–3.75% range at roughly 97%.
  4. Tokyo is flirting with 70,000. The Nikkei 225 is pressing toward the milestone this morning, extending Japan's record run even as US tech wobbles.
  5. Gold ignored the calm. Bullion rose 0.8% to about $4,351 and Citi lifted its near-term target to $4,500 — a sign haven demand is driven by rates, not war.

The Big Picture

The cheaper barrel is the market's clearest verdict on peace. Brent's drop below $80 — a level it last traded in March — is the financial expression of a ceasefire that markets now treat as durable rather than tactical. Through April and May, every flare-up around the Strait of Hormuz was worth several dollars of risk premium; with Washington and Tehran signalling a deal is sealed, that premium is being unwound in a hurry, and crude is roughly a fifth below its 2026 peak. For importers from Europe to Japan, the move is a quiet stimulus: lower energy costs ease the inflation math just as central banks weigh how long to stay restrictive.

What makes this morning unusual is that the disinflationary signal from oil lands on the same day as a Fed decision. A softer barrel ordinarily argues for patience on cuts, yet markets have moved the other way — pricing the June meeting as a near-certain hold and trimming the odds of any 2026 easing. The tell is in the cross-asset tape: the Dow at a record, the Nasdaq lower, gold higher and the dollar flat. That is not a market betting on imminent rate relief; it is one positioning for a Fed that stays put while growth holds up, rotating toward cyclicals and hard assets and away from the long-duration tech that thrives on cheap money.

Markets

Equities: a record with a defensive tone. Wall Street's record streak paused. The Dow closed at an all-time high, up about 0.64%, but the gain masked a rotation: the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.15% to 26,376 and small caps lagged as money rotated out of crowded chip names. The S&P 500 finished a hair lower at 7,548.60, essentially flat. In Asia this morning the mood is firmer — the Nikkei 225 is pressing toward 70,000 for the first time, while Hong Kong's Hang Seng sits a touch higher near 24,460. Europe's bourses open around 11:00 AM GST with falling energy costs as a tailwind and the Fed as an overhang.

Rates & FX: positioning into the print. The 10-year Treasury yield eased about four basis points to 4.43% as traders positioned ahead of the decision, while the dollar index held flat at 99.53. With a hold near-certain, the market's attention is squarely on the updated dot plot and on how Chair Warsh frames the path from here — the first read on whether the new Fed leans more hawkish than the one it replaced.

Commodities & crypto: crude is the headline. Brent shed more than 5% to roughly $79 and WTI dropped toward $77.5, both at three-month lows as the ceasefire bled out the war premium. Gold pushed higher to about $4,351 an ounce, up 0.8%, with Citi lifting its near-term target to $4,500. Bitcoin slipped 0.6% to around $65,700, tracking the cautious risk tone into the Fed.

"A record Dow, a falling Nasdaq and a $79 barrel tell one story: the market is buying the peace and the growth — not the rate cut."

The Fed: A New Chair, A Familiar Hold

Tonight is about tone, not the number. Futures price roughly a 97% chance the FOMC leaves its target range at 3.50–3.75% when it announces at 10:00 PM GST, with the odds of a June cut having collapsed from around 36% earlier this month to near 3% today. The drama sits in the accompanying Summary of Economic Projections and in Kevin Warsh's debut press conference half an hour later. Investors want to know whether the dot plot still pencils in any 2026 easing at all, and whether the new Chair signals a bias toward holding rates higher for longer.

The setup is delicate. Cheaper oil is disinflationary and would normally hand the Fed room to ease, yet a resilient labour market and record equity prices argue for patience. How Warsh resolves that tension — dovish relief or hawkish discipline — will set the dollar and the long end into the close.

The Bottom Line

Peace is showing up in the oil price, growth is showing up in a record Dow, and discipline is showing up in collapsing rate-cut odds — three signals pointing the same way. The risk into tonight is not the Fed's decision, which is all but written, but its posture: if Kevin Warsh confirms the market's hawkish read, the rotation out of tech and into cyclicals and gold has further to run. For now, the cheapest barrel since March is the most important number on the board — and it is telling investors to position for a Fed that can afford to wait.
