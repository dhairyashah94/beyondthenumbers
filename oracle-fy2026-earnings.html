Oracle FY2026: A $638 Billion Promise, Paid For in Cash

Oracle closed its fiscal year with record revenue, 93% cloud-infrastructure growth and the largest order book in enterprise software history. It also burned $23.7 billion in free cash flow building the data centers meant to deliver it — and the stock fell anyway. We go through the print line by line: what the backlog is really made of, where the cash is going, and the three numbers that decide the stock from here.

By Dhairya Shah · Equity Research · 14 min read

Our view Own the backlog, respect the burn

There are quarters where a company beats and the stock falls because the market is in a bad mood. This was not one of those. Oracle reported its fiscal fourth quarter after Wednesday's close and beat on essentially every line that matters — revenue, EPS, cloud growth, backlog — and the shares fell more than 7% in extended trading anyway, into a tape that had already repriced the entire AI-infrastructure complex around a hawkish Fed. The market was not disputing the quarter. It was disputing the funding model.

That distinction is the whole note. Oracle has executed a transformation that, two years ago, almost nobody believed it could: the database-license company of the 1990s is now, by revenue mix, a cloud-infrastructure company growing its core segment at 93% with a contracted order book of $638 billion. The print proves the demand is real. What it cannot yet prove — and what a 4.53% ten-year yield is now demanding proof of — is that the demand converts to cash faster than the build-out consumes it. Until that crossover shows up on the cash flow statement, every Oracle quarter will be graded on two report cards at once.

The quarter: a clean beat on every operating line

Start with what was unambiguously good, because it was most of the print. Fourth-quarter revenue of $19.18 billion rose 21% year-on-year, ahead of the $19.10 billion consensus. Adjusted EPS of $2.03 beat the $1.96 the street expected — and that is the conservative reading; including one-time net investment gains the reported non-GAAP figure was $2.11, which is the number you will see quoted elsewhere. Total cloud revenue grew 47% to $9.9 billion. Cloud infrastructure — OCI, the segment carrying the AI story — grew 93% to $5.8 billion in the quarter and 77% for the full year.

Note the one soft line in the table, because it matters to the story: software license and support — the legacy franchise that funded this company for forty years — was flat-to-down at $24.5 billion. Oracle is not adding a cloud business on top of a growing core. It is replacing its core in flight. That makes the cloud numbers more impressive and the margin for error smaller: there is no second engine if the build-out stalls.

The backlog: extraordinary, and extraordinarily concentrated

Remaining performance obligations — contracted revenue not yet recognized — closed the year at $638 billion. The figure is up 363% year-on-year and rose $85 billion in the fourth quarter alone. There is no precedent for this in enterprise software; it is roughly nine and a half years of FY26 revenue already under contract. This is the number that turned Oracle from a mature-software holding into an AI-infrastructure trade, and it is the single best argument the bulls have.

Now the discipline. Management was candid that the bulk of the increase comes from a small number of very large AI contracts, and that roughly $75 billion of the backlog reflects customer prepayments for GPU capacity or customer-supplied hardware. Read that carefully: a meaningful slice of the most important number in the bull case is (a) concentrated in a handful of counterparties whose own economics are unproven, and (b) tied to hardware whose pricing and supply Oracle does not fully control. RPO is a contract, not cash — it converts on the customer's schedule, at the contract's margin, and only if the counterparty is still standing when the capacity comes online. A $638 billion order book from a diversified base of enterprises is a fortress. A $638 billion order book where the marginal dollar comes from a few AI labs racing each other to train frontier models is a fortress with a concentrated foundation.

"RPO is a promise. CapEx is cash. The entire Oracle debate is the exchange rate between the two."

The mix shift is complete: cloud is half the company

For the first time in its history, Oracle's cloud revenue ($34.0 billion, +39%) exceeded its legacy license-and-support line ($24.5 billion, −1%). Five years ago the strategic question was whether Oracle would ever matter in cloud. That question is now settled — OCI is taking genuine share in AI training and inference workloads, not merely riding the generic cloud tide — and it was settled the expensive way: by building capacity ahead of demand and pricing aggressively against AWS, Azure and Google, leaning on the one asset the hyperscalers cannot replicate, an installed base of enterprise databases and ERP systems that is brutally expensive to leave.

The cost of that pivot shows up one line below revenue: gross margin compressed roughly five points over the year as low-margin, capital-heavy infrastructure scaled faster than high-margin software. This is the textbook hyperscaler pattern — AWS ran years of margin dilution before the operating leverage arrived — but Oracle is running it at a later stage of the rate cycle, with a balance sheet that is already working hard. The bulls' model needs infrastructure gross margin to inflect as utilization rises. The bears' model says GPU fleets depreciate faster than enterprise software ever did, and the margin never fully arrives. FY27 will produce the first real evidence either way.

The catch: $23.7 billion out the door

Here is the price of all of the above. Oracle generated a record $32.0 billion in operating cash flow this year, up 54% — a genuinely excellent result that would headline any normal print. It then spent $55.7 billion on capital expenditure, up 162%, almost entirely on data-center build-out. Free cash flow: negative $23.7 billion, the first materially negative year in the company's modern history.

To bridge the gap, Oracle raised roughly $48 billion in debt and equity during the fiscal year, and management has been explicit that the build-out is not done: net capital expenditure is guided to roughly $70 billion in FY2027, with plans to raise about $40 billion more — including a share sale of around $20 billion. That last item deserves a pause. Oracle spent two decades as one of the great share-count shrinkers in large-cap tech; it is now preparing to issue equity at scale to fund data centers. Combined debt and lease obligations already exceed $160 billion and are climbing. None of this is hidden, reckless or unusual for the AI build-out — Microsoft, Meta and Alphabet are running versions of the same play. The difference is that they fund it from free cash flow. Oracle funds it from the market, which means Oracle's cost of capital — and therefore the Fed — sits inside its equity story in a way it does not for the megacap peers.

That is why Wednesday's macro backdrop mattered so much. With CPI at 4.2%, futures pricing roughly 70% odds of a rate hike by December, and the ten-year at 4.53%, the marginal dollar of AI capex got more expensive on exactly the day Oracle asked the market to fund $40 billion more of it. The stock's 7% after-hours drop was not a verdict on demand. It was the financing bill being marked to market.

The bet management is asking you to underwrite

Guidance is the counter-argument, and it is aggressive. Management reaffirmed $90 billion of revenue for FY2027 — 34% growth, nearly double this year's pace — and nudged non-GAAP EPS guidance to $8.05, ahead of the ~$8.01 consensus. The longer arc from its analyst day: a 31% revenue CAGR and 28% EPS CAGR through FY2030. The thesis compresses to one sentence: the $638 billion backlog converts into profitable revenue fast enough to outrun the capital being consumed to serve it.

Is the guide credible? The backlog says yes on revenue: with $638 billion contracted, hitting $90 billion is substantially a question of how fast Oracle can energize data centers, not whether customers show up. The honest uncertainty is everything below the revenue line — what margin that revenue carries as it lands, and how much more capital it takes to serve it. A company can hit a 34% growth guide and still disappoint if the incremental margin comes in low and the FY28 capex guide goes up again. Watch the spread between revenue growth and operating-income growth each quarter; that spread is the thesis, reported quarterly.

What can break the thesis

Conversion risk. The gap between RPO and revenue is a schedule, and Oracle does not fully control it. If the large AI customers defer capacity — because training budgets tighten, because their own funding gets expensive, because the model race consolidates — the backlog stays on the slide deck while the depreciation from $55.7 billion of capex lands on the income statement on time.

Funding-market risk. Oracle plans to raise roughly $40 billion in FY27. In a market pricing Fed hikes, every 50 basis points of yield moves the economics of that raise. The equity story now has a credit story inside it; watch the bond spreads as closely as the stock.

Margin risk. Five points of gross-margin compression is tolerable as a transition cost. It is a thesis-breaker if it is structural — if AI infrastructure is simply a lower-quality business than the software it is replacing. Utilization data and infrastructure gross margin in the next two quarters are the tell.

Concentration risk. A small number of AI counterparties account for the marginal backlog dollar. Any public stumble at one of them — a funding round that fails, a training program cancelled — would reprice the entire RPO multiple overnight, regardless of Oracle's own execution.

The bottom line

This was a genuinely strong print sold into a market that has stopped paying for growth funded by borrowed money. Both halves of that sentence are true, and the stock's reaction reflects the second, not the first. Operationally, Oracle just posted the best evidence yet that it belongs in the first tier of AI infrastructure: 93% IaaS growth, a completed mix shift, and an order book without precedent. Financially, it is running the most leveraged version of the hyperscaler build-out at the worst point in the rate cycle, and it has told you plainly that the burn continues through FY27.

Our posture: own the backlog, respect the burn. The three numbers that decide the stock from here, in order: the pace of RPO-to-revenue conversion (management's quarterly "cloud revenue growth" against that $90 billion guide), infrastructure gross margin as utilization scales, and the terms of the FY27 financing — especially the ~$20 billion equity sale. The first checkpoint is Q1 FY2027 results on September 10, with the AI World investor day on October 28 behind it. And next week's FOMC matters more to this name than to almost any other megacap: Oracle has, by choice, tied its equity story to the price of money. Until free cash flow inflects, it trades like what it has become — a magnificent operating story with a bond market sitting on its shoulder.
