A Record in Tokyo, a Hawkish Fed in Washington — and Wall Street Reopens Tonight Into the Gap

US desks have been dark since Thursday's record S&P close. They return this evening Dubai time to a Fed that is now flirting with a rate hike, oil still hostage to the Strait of Hormuz, and a Nikkei that punched through 72,000 overnight.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Wall Street reopens tonight. US markets trade for the first time since Thursday's record close of 7,500.58 on the S&P; Friday's Juneteenth holiday and the weekend kept desks dark.
  2. The Fed has flipped hawkish. New Chair Kevin Warsh held the target range at 3.50–3.75% on June 17, but the dot plot now tilts toward tightening — futures price a 25bp hike as early as October.
  3. Tokyo set a record. The Nikkei 225 jumped 1.95% past 72,000 this morning, even as Hong Kong's Hang Seng fell 1.74% and mainland China barely budged.
  4. Oil is the swing factor. Brent sits near $80 with Strait of Hormuz transit still contested after US–Iran talks in Geneva were abruptly postponed on Friday.
  5. Inflation is the week's marquee. May core PCE — the Fed's preferred gauge — lands Thursday, with April's 3.8% headline still uncomfortably above target.

The Big Picture

For most of the past two years, the only question about the Federal Reserve was the timing of its next cut. That question is gone. In Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Chair, the FOMC not only held at 3.50–3.75% by a unanimous 12–0 vote — it erased the lone 2026 cut from its projections and pushed the median dot toward a hike. Fed funds futures now price a quarter-point increase by October, with nothing further through 2027. That is a regime change, and it is the lens through which every other number this morning should be read.

What the market is pricing is not a growth scare but a re-acceleration of inflation the Fed feels compelled to lean against. April's PCE ran at 3.8% year-on-year and a punishing 6% annualized over three months, fed in part by energy costs tied to the Gulf conflict. That is why the curve can hold a 4.46% ten-year yield, a 100.8 dollar, and gold above $4,280 at the same time — bonds reflect a Fed that won't ease, the dollar reflects rate support, and gold reflects the inflation-plus-geopolitics hedge that a hawkish Fed cannot fully neutralize. Equities, for their part, closed Thursday at records, betting that earnings can outrun a higher-for-longer policy rate. The reopening tonight tests how much of that optimism survived a long weekend of Hormuz headlines.

Markets

Equities: records, but narrow. The S&P 500's 1.08% Thursday advance to 7,500.58 and the Nasdaq's 1.91% surge to 26,517.93 capped a week in which the index shook off an early-June semiconductor wobble — recall the Nasdaq's 4% single-day plunge on June 4 — to print fresh highs. Breadth was narrower than the headline suggests: the Dow added just 0.31%, leaving megacap tech doing the heavy lifting again. In Asia this morning, Japan led with the Nikkei past 72,000 while the Hang Seng's 1.74% drop and a flat CSI 300 underscored China's persistent underperformance.

Rates & FX: a symmetric hawkish story. The ten-year Treasury yield holds at 4.46%, near the middle of its month-long 4.42–4.69% range, as a Fed that won't cut keeps the front end anchored. The dollar index sits at 100.83 after the ECB's surprise June 11 hike to 2.25% — its first since 2023 — gave the euro a floor; EUR/USD trades around 1.143. Both central banks are now leaning hawkish, leaving the dollar's edge to come from growth and safe-haven flows rather than rate differentials alone.

Commodities & crypto: a premium that won't drain. Brent settled near $80.57 on Friday, up 0.9%, after US–Iran talks in Geneva were abruptly postponed; Tehran's move to require insurance for Strait of Hormuz transit keeps a risk premium embedded even as ships pass unmolested. Gold near $4,283 reflects the same anxiety from the other side. Bitcoin, by contrast, looks unloved at roughly $65,000 — well below its 2025 peak above $71,000 — a hawkish-Fed casualty as real yields stay elevated and liquidity tightens.

"For the first time in years, the question in Washington isn't when the Fed cuts — it's whether it has to hike again."

The Warsh Fed Draws a Harder Line

The dot plot is where the regime change shows up most starkly. A year ago the median participant penciled in cuts; in June, the committee split nine-to-eight-to-one in favor of at least one hike against no change, with a single lonely dove. Warsh's debut press conference reinforced the message, stressing price stability over the labor-market caveats that defined the prior chair. The market took the hint immediately — pricing that had assumed a steady hold for the rest of 2026 now embeds a quarter-point hike by October. For CFOs modeling 2027 financing, the cost-of-capital assumption just moved the wrong way.

The Bottom Line

The record highs on Thursday were earned in a world where the Fed's next move was assumed to be down. It isn't anymore. When New York reopens this evening Dubai time, the test is whether equity bulls can keep paying record multiples for earnings while the policy rate's next stop is a hike and a barrel of Brent still answers to events in the Gulf. Cheap optimism met an expensive backdrop over the long weekend — tonight we learn which one blinks.
