easyJet (LSE: EZJ) — What Is the Business Worth When a War Sets the Fuel Bill?

A live takeover, a winter half that lost £552m before tax, and a fuel line written by the Middle East conflict. easyJet is the cleanest case study going for the question every valuation actually turns on: not what a company earned, but what it can sustainably earn once the one-offs are stripped out — and why adjusted EBITDA is where that argument is won or lost.

The Thesis in Five Lines

  1. The reported number is almost useless for valuation. easyJet's winter half is structurally loss-making before any shock; layer a war-driven fuel spike on top and the £552m pre-tax loss tells you little about what the business actually earns through a cycle.
  2. Valuation runs on maintainable earnings. Both a discounted-cash-flow and a multiple approach price the steady-state earning power of the asset — so genuinely one-off costs have to be identified and stripped out before you capitalize anything.
  3. The fuel spike is the textbook one-off — but only partly. A war moving Brent is exogenous and non-recurring; the trouble is that fuel is volatile every year, so the line between "extraordinary shock" and "normal volatility you must bear" is a judgement, not a fact.
  4. Identifying the add-backs is the hard, contestable part. Adjustment creep, hedging noise, knock-on demand effects, and growth-versus-exceptional ambiguity mean the "underlying" figure is an argument — which is exactly why adjusted EBITDA, not statutory profit, is the battleground.
  5. That argument is the takeover. Castlelake's rejected bids and the board's "undervalues the company" defence are, at bottom, two different views of normalized EBITDA and the multiple it deserves.

Two businesses, one winter, and a war in the fuel line

easyJet is really two companies wearing one ticker. The first is a low-cost airline — capital-heavy, fuel-exposed, and seasonal to the bone: it loses money flying half-empty planes through the European winter and makes it back across the summer peak. The second is easyJet holidays, an asset-light package-holiday business that is growing fast, earning real margin, and behaving nothing like the airline. In the six months to 31 March 2026, the airline ran an operating loss of £581m while holidays grew earnings 50% to £48m on a 22% rise in customers. Same company, opposite signals.

Onto that already-loss-making winter landed an external shock. The Middle East conflict pushed jet fuel sharply higher and clouded near-term demand, and easyJet said as much: management attributed roughly £180m of year-on-year deterioration in airline losses to a combination of higher fuel, strategic start-up costs at its new Milan and Rome bases, competitive overcapacity in some beach markets, and cost inflation. The headline outcome was a £552m pre-tax loss — but that headline blends three very different kinds of cost, and a valuation has to take them apart.

Why you can't value the number the company reported

Every valuation method, underneath the spreadsheet, does the same thing: it estimates what the business can earn on a sustainable, mid-cycle basis and capitalizes that. A discounted-cash-flow model projects a maintainable free-cash-flow stream and discounts it; an EV/EBITDA multiple takes a normalized EBITDA and applies a market rating. Both fall apart if you feed them a number distorted by something that won't happen again. A war-driven fuel spike is the definition of something that — in any given form — won't repeat: it tells you about geopolitics in the spring of 2026, not about easyJet's earning power.

So the analyst's first job is not arithmetic, it's normalization: rebuilding a clean, maintainable earnings figure by adding back the costs that are genuinely non-recurring and leaving in the ones that are simply the business being itself. The mechanism people reach for is a bridge from the reported result to "underlying" or adjusted earnings — and the most-watched rung on that bridge is adjusted EBITDA, because it strips out the financing structure and the depreciation schedule (both of which vary hugely across leased-vs-owned airline fleets) and gets closest to maintainable cash earning power.

The hard part: which costs are actually extraordinary?

Adding back a "war fuel spike" sounds simple on a slide. In practice, deciding what counts as extraordinary is the single most contestable judgement in the whole exercise — and it is where good analysis separates from wishful thinking. Five problems recur, and easyJet hits all of them.

Pull those four rows apart and the difficulties become concrete:

1
One-off versus ever-present volatility. Fuel spikes are "extraordinary" individually, yet fuel is volatile every single year. Strip out every bad fuel year and you build a fantasy business that never pays a realistic energy bill. The discipline is to remove the war-driven excess over a normal hedged cost — not the whole fuel line — which requires a defensible view of what "normal" is.
2
Adjustment creep. Management is structurally incentivized to classify anything bad as "exceptional" and anything good as "underlying." Restructuring charges that recur annually, integration costs that never end, perennial "one-off" provisions — left unpoliced, the adjusted number drifts ever further above reality. Skepticism is the analyst's core job here.
3
Knock-on effects are hard to isolate. A fuel shock doesn't only raise the fuel line. It dents demand, shifts forward bookings, moves FX, and changes the value of hedges — so the "pure" extraordinary cost is entangled with second-order effects that are partly structural. Clean separation is an estimate, never a fact.
4
Hedging muddies the print. easyJet was about 72% hedged for H2 FY26 at ~$726/tonne, so the reported fuel cost is already part-insulated, and mark-to-market movements on hedges can swing the P&L for reasons unrelated to operations. The cash cost, the hedged cost, and the reported cost are three different numbers — and only one of them belongs in a normalized figure.
5
Growth spend versus exceptional cost. The Milan and Rome start-up costs depress profit today but are a deliberate investment, not a misfortune. Whether to add them back depends on whether you are valuing the business as it stands or as it is becoming — a framing choice that materially moves the answer.
"Statutory profit tells you what happened. Adjusted EBITDA is an argument about what normally happens — and in a takeover, whoever wins that argument sets the price."

Why adjusted EBITDA becomes the battleground

Put the pieces together and you can see why, for a business like this, adjusted EBITDA is not an accounting footnote — it is the whole valuation. Run the bridge from the reported winter loss down through depreciation and the one-off add-backs and you arrive at a figure that is still negative (winter always is) but dramatically less alarming than the statutory headline. That normalized figure, annualized across the seasonal cycle and paired with the cash-generative summer, is what any acquirer actually capitalizes.

The point is the method and the size of the gap — roughly £140m between reported and adjusted EBITDA on these assumptions — not the precise endpoint.

Three reasons this rung carries the weight. First, EBITDA neutralizes the financing and accounting choices that make airlines hard to compare — owned versus leased fleets, different depreciation lives, IFRS 16 lease treatment — so it travels across peers and across a buyer's leveraged model. Second, EV/EBITDA is the multiple the market and acquirers actually quote for the sector, so the normalized EBITDA number is the input that gets multiplied into a price. Third — and this is the catch — adjusted EBITDA is a non-GAAP figure with no single legal definition: management decides what "adjusted" means. That is precisely why it is contestable, and why a careful reader treats every add-back as a claim to be tested rather than a fact to be accepted.

What it means for the bid

This is not abstract. Castlelake has approached easyJet four times — at 560p, 600p, 625p, and most recently a sweetened proposal valuing the company at roughly £4.93bn — and the board has rejected each as undervaluing the business, while granting more time and limited diligence to test a higher number. Strip away the choreography and the disagreement is exactly the one this note is about: what is easyJet's normalized, mid-cycle earning power, and what multiple does it deserve?

A bidder running leveraged-buyout math wants the lowest defensible adjusted EBITDA — it keeps the entry price down and lets the war-scarred reported numbers do the arguing. The board wants the highest defensible version — fuel spike removed, holidays' growth capitalized generously, summer cash flow front-and-centre, winter losses dismissed as seasonal and exceptional. Both are building the same bridge from the same reported loss; they just stop adding back at different rungs of the ladder in Chart 3. The gap between their two "underlying" numbers, multiplied out, is most of the gap between 625p and whatever clears the deal.

That is the practical lesson of valuing a company through an extraordinary shock. The reported loss is loud but uninformative; the real work is the disciplined, skeptical reconstruction of maintainable earnings — and the moment money is on the table, that reconstruction stops being an academic exercise and becomes the negotiation itself.

What could change the picture

1
Summer is the proof. The whole normalized case rests on the peak half delivering. A weak summer — on demand, pricing, or another fuel leg higher — would validate the bears and shrink the maintainable EBITDA everyone is arguing over.
2
Fuel is structurally recurring. If geopolitical risk keeps Brent elevated, "one-off" fuel spikes start to look like a permanent feature — and the case for adding them back weakens with every repeat.
3
The adjustments are judgement, not fact. Every figure in the bridges here is an estimate. Reasonable analysts will allocate the £180m deterioration differently, and small changes in the add-backs move the adjusted number — and therefore the implied value — meaningfully.
4
Deal path is uncertain. Castlelake must firm up or walk away; ownership-restriction rules complicate the bidding group; and a collapse of the talks would reprice the equity back toward fundamentals fast. The premium in the shares is contingent, not banked.
5
Holidays carries the story. Much of the bull case is the asset-light holidays arm compounding. Any stumble in delivery there removes the cleanest part of the valuation and throws weight back onto the seasonal, fuel-exposed airline.

The bottom line

easyJet's winter print is a near-perfect teaching case. A £552m pre-tax loss looks catastrophic and tells you almost nothing, because it folds a structural off-season, a deliberate growth investment, and a war-driven fuel shock into a single line. Valuation lives one layer down — in the patient separation of what won't recur from what will, and in the adjusted EBITDA that separation produces. That number is not handed to you; it is built, defended, and contested. With a bidder at the door, the contest has a price tag, and the side that wins the argument over normalized earnings will, in the end, set the value of the company.
