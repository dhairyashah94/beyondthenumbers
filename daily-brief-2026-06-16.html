The Peace Rally Made Records. Now the Fed Gets a Vote.

Wall Street priced a war out of the market in a single session — the Dow closed at a record, the Nasdaq had its best day in months — and Brent now sits near $84, down a quarter from its wartime peak. This morning Asia is pausing, not chasing, because the rally's next test is not in the Gulf but in Washington, where the Fed's two-day meeting begins today.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. A record on the relief. The Dow closed at an all-time high (+0.92%), the S&P 500 added 1.65% to 7,554, and the Nasdaq jumped 3.07% as the confirmed US–Iran deal pulled the war premium out of every risk asset.
  2. Oil keeps bleeding. Brent fell another ~4% toward $84 — roughly a quarter below its mid-May peak above $108 — with WTI near $81 as traders price oil flowing through a reopened Hormuz once the deal is signed Friday.
  3. Asia takes a breath. After Monday's 5% surge to a record, the Nikkei added a slimmer 0.8% this morning while Hong Kong and Sydney slipped — a consolidation, not a reversal, ahead of the Fed.
  4. The Fed convenes today. The FOMC's two-day meeting begins this afternoon GST, with the decision and all-important dot plot landing Wednesday; a hold is near-certain at the 3.50–3.75% range.
  5. The data already cracked. Monday's Empire State factory gauge fell 14 points to 5.7, a reminder that the oil shock bruised activity even as the ceasefire now lifts the mood.

The Big Picture

Monday was the cleanest one-day repricing of a geopolitical risk in years. With the Strait of Hormuz set to reopen and the naval blockade lifted, the market did in hours what it had refused to do for three months: mark the war premium back out of crude, equities and volatility simultaneously. Brent's slide toward $84 — a near-25% retreat from its wartime high — is the engine. Cheaper energy flows straight into the earnings math for oil-importing Asia and into Europe's beaten-down travel, consumer and industrial names, which is why the Stoxx 600 also printed a record Monday. The VIX at 16.2 says the market now sees the tail it feared all spring as substantially closed.

But notice what Asia did overnight: it stopped chasing. The hand-off is underway from a story the market can price in a weekend — peace — to one it cannot, which is whether the Federal Reserve will treat cheaper oil as permission to ease or as a sideshow next to 4.2% inflation. That is the real significance of this morning's pause. The relief rally has already happened; from here, the path runs through Wednesday's dot plot, and a market sitting at fresh records has priced in a Fed that does not stand in the way. Any hint otherwise, and the same speed that built these gains can unwind them.

Markets

Equities: the year's most decisive session. The S&P 500 closed up 1.65% at 7,554.29, the Nasdaq Composite leapt 3.07% to 26,683.94, and the Dow added 469 points to a record 51,671.03. Semiconductor and AI names led — Tokyo's Murata jumped 17.6% and SoftBank 10.3% in the same wave — as lower energy costs and fading geopolitical risk reopened the high-multiple trade. This morning the Nikkei edged up 0.8% to extend its record while Hong Kong's Hang Seng and Australia's ASX 200 slipped fractionally, the classic pre-Fed crouch.

Rates & FX: a disinflationary tell. The 10-year Treasury yield eased to 4.47%, down on the session — a notable move given the risk-on backdrop, and a sign bond traders see cheaper oil as disinflationary cover for the Fed. The dollar index was effectively flat at 99.42; with havens unwinding and yields slipping, the greenback's lack of follow-through is itself a tell that the next catalyst is monetary, not geopolitical.

Commodities & crypto: crude prices a functioning Hormuz. Brent fell another 3.95% to $83.88 and WTI to near $81, both at roughly three-month lows as the market prices a functioning strait. Gold slipped just 0.22% to $4,341.90, holding firm despite the risk rally — a hint that central-bank and inflation-hedge demand persists. Bitcoin added 1.32% to $66,348, riding the same liquidity and risk impulse that lifted equities.

The Fed Walks In With Cover — and a Problem

The oil collapse hands the Fed a gift it did not have a week ago: a powerful disinflationary impulse arriving exactly as the Committee sits down. Brent near $84 versus above $108 in mid-May is the single biggest swing in the inflation outlook this quarter, and it lands while Monday's Empire State survey shows factory momentum already cooling. Yet the problem is unchanged — the most recent CPI ran at 4.2%, the hottest in three years, and the funds rate at 3.50–3.75% still sits below it. A hold on Wednesday is all but certain; the market's entire focus is the Summary of Economic Projections. If the median dot signals that cheaper oil buys room to resume cutting, the record run extends. If it signals high-for-longer regardless, a market priced for relief has to re-rate.

The Bottom Line

The war is priced out; the Fed is not. Monday's records were the easy half of this story — a relief rally always is — and the pause across Asia overnight is the market admitting as much. The harder question lands Wednesday: whether a Fed staring at 4.2% inflation lets a quarter-off oil price do its work, or tells investors that the cost of money stays high no matter how quiet the Gulf gets. Peace moved the tape. The dot plot will decide whether it holds.
