Wall Street Caps Its Best Quarter Since 2020 as the Chip Trade Swallows a Hawkish Fed

A second record close on the Dow and a 21% quarter for the Nasdaq leave equities priced for AI perfection — even as new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh signals the next move is a hike, not a cut.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. A quarter for the record books. The S&P 500 rose 14.8% and the Nasdaq 21.4% in Q2 — their best three months since the 2020 rebound — while the Dow booked its strongest first half in five years.
  2. Chips did nearly all the lifting. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index surged 87.8% last quarter, its best on record, with Micron and Intel more than tripling as the AI trade broadened past Nvidia.
  3. The Fed has turned hawkish. Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair ended with rates on hold and a nod to possible hikes; futures now price no 2026 cut, a reversal from spring.
  4. Oil is leaking its risk premium. Brent slipped near $72 as US–Iran talks in Doha eased Gulf supply fears; UAE bourses are holding most of their post-truce gains.
  5. Bitcoin broke $60,000. The token fell below that line for the first time since 2024 — roughly 50% under its $126k peak — as speculative appetite concentrates in equities.

The Big Picture

The second quarter did not merely rise — it re-rated an entire complex. The chip index nearly doubled in three months, adding some $2 trillion of combined value to Micron, Intel and AMD alone, as investors stopped treating the AI build-out as a Nvidia story and started pricing it as an industry-wide capital-spending supercycle. That is why the Nasdaq's 21% quarter matters more than the Dow's fresh record: the market is no longer paying up for a handful of megacaps but for the entire supply chain that feeds them, from foundries to memory to power. When breadth like that accompanies new highs, the rally is harder to dismiss as narrow froth.

The tension sits underneath. Equities have staged their best quarter since 2020 into a Fed that has explicitly signalled its next move could be up. Warsh's debut statement leaned hawkish on inflation, and rate futures have swung from pricing a cut to entertaining an October hike. Stocks are, in effect, betting that AI-driven earnings growth can outrun a higher-for-longer funds rate — that the discount rate stops mattering when the growth numerator compounds fast enough. That is a coherent thesis, but it is also a demanding one: it leaves little room for a soft data print or a hawkish surprise. This morning's jobs indicators are the first test of whether the macro will cooperate.

Markets

Equities. The Dow closed at 52,319.20 for a second straight record, up 0.26%, but the tape belonged to technology: the Nasdaq jumped 1.52% to 26,213.72 and the S&P 500 added 0.79% to 7,449.36. Europe echoed the tone overnight, with the Stoxx 600 up 0.9% and up roughly 9% for its best quarter since October 2020. This morning in Asia the mood is steadier — Shanghai edged up 0.2% to about 4,103, while Tokyo and Hong Kong traded mixed after Wall Street's lead.

Rates & FX. The 10-year Treasury yield held near 4.44%, a level that captures the market's new reality: a Fed done cutting for now, with the balance of risk tilted toward a hike. The dollar index firmed 0.2% to 101.17 and the euro slipped to $1.1407 as traders parsed the ECB's Sintra forum, where Christine Lagarde, Warsh and the Bank of England's Andrew Bailey share a panel today.

Commodities & crypto. Brent eased about 1% to roughly $72.40 and WTI sat near $70.40 as US–Iran negotiations in Doha drained the Gulf risk premium — a relief for importers, a drag for producers. Gold slipped 0.6% to about $3,984, and bitcoin's break below $60,000 — its first since 2024 and some 50% off record highs — underscored how narrowly this year's risk-taking has been channelled into AI equities.

"Equities are priced for an AI build-out that never stumbles and a Fed that never hikes — the second quarter delivered the first and dared the second."

The Fed's Hawkish Pivot Meets a Record Tape

What makes this quarter unusual is not the size of the gains but the backdrop against which they were earned. Rallies of this magnitude typically arrive when the Fed is easing; this one came as the central bank, under a new and more hawkish chair, closed the door on 2026 cuts and cracked open the possibility of a hike. Goldman Sachs has pushed its next-cut call out to 2027. In an ordinary cycle, that repricing of the discount rate would have compressed valuations. Instead, the earnings power investors ascribe to the AI build-out has been rising faster than the rate they use to discount it — so multiples expanded anyway.

The chart below frames the imbalance. When one industry index nearly doubles in a single quarter while the broad market gains in the teens, the rally's fate is tethered to a single thesis. That concentration is the market's greatest strength today and its clearest vulnerability if this morning's labour data — or Warsh's tone at Sintra — forces a rethink of the rate path.

The Bottom Line

The best quarter in six years was built on a single, powerful bet: that artificial intelligence will generate earnings fast enough to render a hawkish Fed irrelevant. For three months that bet paid spectacularly, and the breadth beneath the chip rally suggests it is more than a mirage. But records set into a tightening bias leave no cushion. The half-year mark is not a victory lap — it is the moment the market's conviction gets priced against the data. This morning's jobs numbers and Warsh's words at Sintra will tell us whether the second half starts with confirmation or a reckoning.
