Warsh's Hawkish Debut Tightens the Screws as Hormuz Reopens

A new Fed chair pencils in a 2026 rate hike, Wall Street's longest tech run breaks, and a Gulf peace deal pulls oil to three-month lows — Dubai wakes to a market repricing two shocks at once.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The Fed blinked hawkish. Kevin Warsh's first FOMC held rates at 3.50–3.75% but lifted the median 2026 dot to 3.8% from 3.4%, signalling at least one hike still to come this year.
  2. Wall Street's rally cracked. The S&P 500 fell 1.21% and the Nasdaq 1.34% — every sector lower, communications services down 2.91% — as the cheap-money thesis took a direct hit.
  3. Oil keeps sliding. Brent slipped toward $78, a fifth straight down day and a three-month low, as the US–Iran deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz promises a flood of returning barrels.
  4. Gold won't quit. Bullion held near $4,326 even with the dollar firmer and yields up — a tell that the bid is structural, not just a rate trade.
  5. Asia shrugged, for now. Tokyo opened higher (Nikkei +0.37%) and the yen slid past its intervention line, with the BOJ's hike to 1% colliding with a suddenly hawkish Fed.

The Big Picture

For two years the market's central assumption has been that the next move from the Federal Reserve would be down. Overnight on Wall Street, Kevin Warsh tore that assumption up on his first day in the chair. The committee held the target range at 3.50–3.75%, as expected, but the Summary of Economic Projections did the talking: the median year-end fed funds dot jumped to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, nine of eighteen officials now pencil in at least one hike this year, and six see more than one. The statement itself was cut by more than half, stripped of the dovish hedges that had cushioned every meeting since the easing cycle began. Warsh, pointedly, declined to submit a dot of his own — a signal of optionality that leaves the market guessing about how hard the new regime intends to lean.

What is being repriced is not a single hike but the floor under risk assets. The two-year yield, the maturity most sensitive to policy, leapt roughly 16 basis points to 4.22%, and the dollar surged. Equities did the arithmetic instantly: a higher-for-longer — or higher-from-here — funds rate compresses the multiples that have carried megacap tech to records, which is why the Nasdaq led the drop and the VIX spiked 12% to 18.44. The cruel irony is that the inflation forcing the Fed's hand was largely imported, born of the Hormuz energy shock now unwinding. Warsh is tightening into a supply story that is already reversing — betting that credibility today is worth more than the growth it costs.

Markets

Equities: broad and orderly, not panicked. The S&P 500's 1.21% slide to 7,420.10 unwound roughly a week of gains, with the Nasdaq's 1.34% drop to 26,021.66 led by communications and consumer-cyclical names most exposed to discount-rate math. The Dow shed 507 points to 51,492.55. In Asia this morning the reaction is more measured: the Nikkei opened up 0.37% near 70,164 and Korea's Kospi added 0.2%, while Hong Kong's Hang Seng — down 1.4% to 24,494 in its prior session — remains the regional laggard. Europe's Stoxx 600 closed muted near 638 ahead of the decision and opens around 11:00 AM GST into a changed rates picture.

Rates & FX: the front end believes him. The repricing is cleanest at the short end: two-year yields jumped about 16 bps to 4.22% and the 10-year firmed to roughly 4.46%, flattening the curve as the market accepts a higher policy floor. The dollar index pushed back above 100, and the yen slid through the line that triggered intervention earlier this year — an awkward outcome for a Bank of Japan that only last week raised its policy rate to 1%, the highest since 1995. Two hawkish central banks pulling in tandem leaves carry trades and the yen as the pressure valve to watch.

Commodities & crypto: supply is coming back. Brent's grind toward $78 — a fifth consecutive decline and a three-month low — is the market pricing the return of Gulf supply, with WTI off 1.7% near $75.50. The IEA had pegged the Hormuz closure at a 14 million barrel-a-day shortfall; its unwinding is now the dominant disinflationary force. Gold's refusal to fall, holding near $4,326 despite a firmer dollar and higher real yields, signals demand that is structural rather than tactical. Bitcoin drifted to about $65,800, range-bound and notably absent from the safe-haven bid.

The Dot Plot Heard Around the World

The single number that moved markets was the median year-end fed funds projection, lifted to 3.8% from 3.4% in March. That 40-basis-point shift converts a committee that was debating the pace of cuts into one openly weighing a hike. The driver, per the projections, is a 2026 PCE inflation forecast revised up to 3.6% — energy and supply distortions from the Middle East conflict feeding through — set against a labour market still adding jobs. Fed-funds futures now assign a meaningful probability, by some measures above 70%, to at least one hike before year-end, a stance that was close to unthinkable a month ago. The deeper signal is institutional: by halving the statement and withholding his own dot, Warsh is reshaping how the Fed communicates, prizing flexibility over the forward guidance markets had grown to lean on.

The Bottom Line

Dubai wakes to a market caught between two shocks moving in opposite directions: a Gulf peace that is pulling the inflationary energy spike out of the system, and a Fed chair who chose this moment to declare that the inflation fight is not over. Warsh's first act was to remove the safety net under risk assets — and the front end of the curve believes him. Until the labour data crack or oil's slide convinces the Fed the disinflation is real, the path of least resistance is a firmer dollar, a heavier equity tape, and a gold market that keeps telling you something the dot plot won't.
