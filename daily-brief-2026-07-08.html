Hormuz Erupts and the AI Trade Cracks — Markets Absorb Two Shocks at Once

Brent jumped above $76 after the US hit more than 80 Iranian targets and Tehran answered with strikes on American bases in Bahrain and Kuwait. Seoul fell 3.3% this morning as the chip rout entered a third day. Tonight: minutes from Kevin Warsh's first FOMC meeting.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The Hormuz truce is fraying fast. After Iranian missiles hit a Qatari LNG carrier and a Saudi crude tanker in the Strait of Hormuz, US Central Command struck more than 80 Iranian targets — including IRGC boats, Kharg Island and Bandar Abbas — and Tehran retaliated overnight against US bases in Bahrain and Kuwait, threatening a "crushing response."
  2. Oil is repricing the strait, not a war. Brent settled 3% higher at $74.16 and pushed to $76.14 as the strikes landed; WTI traded near $72 this morning. Washington also revoked Iran's oil-sale licenses, giving buyers until July 17 to wind down.
  3. The chip rout has a third leg. Samsung's Q2 operating profit rose 19-fold, yet the stock fell over 6% on Tuesday and another 4.3% this morning; the Kospi plunged 3.3% and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index lost more than 4% overnight after reports DeepSeek is designing its own inference chip.
  4. Warsh's first minutes land tonight at 10:00 PM GST. Futures price roughly a 78% chance the Fed holds on July 28–29 — and a 22% chance it hikes, after the dot plot tilted toward tightening despite June payrolls of just 57,000.
  5. China is the quiet soft spot. Shanghai closed Tuesday at 3,990, a near one-month low, after the World Bank trimmed its 2026 China growth forecast to 4.4% against Beijing's 4.5–5.0% target.

The Big Picture

The remarkable thing about the last 24 hours is not what moved — it is what didn't. The United States conducted its largest strike on Iran since the spring war, Tehran fired on American installations in two Gulf states, and yet the VIX closed at 15.57 and US equity futures were little changed into the Asian morning. The market is making a specific, narrow bet: that this exchange is about enforcing rules of passage through the Strait of Hormuz, not about closing it. Brent at $76 — up sharply, but still far below crisis-era peaks — prices a risk premium on 20% of the world's oil traffic, not an interruption of it. The July 17 wind-down window on Iranian oil sales reads as calibrated pressure rather than embargo-by-ambush.

The deeper repricing is happening in technology. Samsung grew operating profit nineteen-fold on AI memory demand and was sold anyway — twice. When results that strong cannot hold a bid, the market is no longer trading earnings; it is trading the durability of the capex cycle behind them. Reports that DeepSeek is designing its own inference chip crystallize the fear: that the biggest buyers of AI silicon become its competitors, and that today's shortage pricing becomes tomorrow's glut. A 4%-plus drop in the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index, Micron below $900, and a 3.3% plunge in Seoul this morning are the sound of that thesis being stress-tested — with a Fed that might hike into it.

Markets

Equities. Overnight on Wall Street the S&P 500 fell 0.45% to 7,503.85 and the Nasdaq lost 1.16% to 25,818.69; the Dow touched a record intraday high before fading 0.25% to 52,925.15. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index dropped more than 4%, with Micron sliding below $900. This morning in Seoul the Kospi plunged 3.3% to 7,400 — its lowest since late May, a day after a Samsung-led rout triggered a circuit breaker — while Tokyo's Nikkei opened down 1.3% at 67,342. Samsung fell a further 4.3% and SK Hynix 4.8%; Hong Kong and Shanghai, which closed Tuesday at 23,497 (−0.5%) and 3,990 (−1.3%) respectively, open into the same crosswinds this morning. Europe's Stoxx 600 slipped 0.7% on Tuesday and reopens at 11:00 AM GST facing a very different oil price.

Rates & FX. The 10-year Treasury yield edged up to 4.50%, a two-week high, with the 30-year holding above 5% — notable firmness given soft June payrolls, and a sign the market is taking the Fed's hawkish dot plot seriously. Futures now price the policy path drifting toward roughly 3.8% by October and near 4% by year-end. The dollar index sat near 100.93, steady, as safe-haven flows and hike risk offset each other.

Commodities & crypto. Brent settled 3% higher at $74.16, then ran to $76.14 as news of the US strikes broke; WTI traded near $72 in Asia this morning. The striking anomaly is gold: down about 1% to roughly $4,114 despite missiles flying — firm yields and a steady dollar are outweighing the geopolitics. Bitcoin slipped 0.7% to about $63,550, behaving like the risk asset it is rather than the haven its promoters describe.

"A 15-handle VIX against burning tankers in Hormuz is the market saying it believes the strait stays open. That is a bet, not a fact."

The Fed: Warsh's First Minutes

Tonight at 10:00 PM GST the Fed releases minutes from Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Chair — and rarely have minutes carried this much signal risk. The June dot plot shifted toward a possible 2026 hike, an extraordinary posture against a labor market that added just 57,000 jobs in June versus 115,000 expected, with April and May revised down. CME futures put the odds of a July 28–29 hold near 78% and a hike near 22%; prediction markets on Polymarket and Kalshi are more confident of a hold, above 85%. The tension is plain: the committee is signaling it fears persistent core inflation more than a cooling jobs market, and tonight's text will show how broadly that conviction is shared. For CFOs, the practical read is that the era of penciling in mechanical cuts is over — funding plans should tolerate a policy rate near 4% into 2027. An oil shock that feeds headline inflation only sharpens the committee's dilemma.

The Bottom Line

Two premiums got repriced overnight: the world put a war-risk surcharge back on Gulf oil, and a durability discount on the AI trade. Only one of them can be resolved by diplomacy. If Hormuz stays open, $76 Brent fades; but if Samsung's nineteen-fold profit growth can't hold a bid, the question every allocator should sit with this morning is not whether the chips rally back — it's what the rest of the market is worth if AI capex is the only thing that has been holding it up.
