Asia's AI Unwind Lands on Wall Street's Doorstep as Dubai Wakes

A Seoul-led rout erased hundreds of billions in tech value on Friday. Wall Street steadied but couldn't rally — and this morning in Tokyo the only question that matters is whether the correction is over, or just pausing for breath.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Asia took the blow. Friday's AI sell-off hit hardest where the rally was steepest — Seoul's KOSPI sank 7.7% and tripped its circuit breaker again, Tokyo's Nikkei fell 4.4% to 69,360, and Taiwan's Taiex lost 3.3%.
  2. Apple lit the fuse. A 15–25% price hike across Mac, iPad and home devices sent Apple down 6.1% on Thursday, and reports that OpenAI will delay its IPO to 2027 knocked SoftBank nearly 13% lower.
  3. Oil keeps sliding. Brent slipped to roughly $72 and WTI to $68.86 — the lowest since February — as a US-Iran détente reopens Hormuz traffic and restores Gulf exports toward pre-war volumes.
  4. Gold is the refuge. Bullion firmed 0.7% to $4,063 an ounce even as the dollar eased, the clearest sign capital is rotating to safety rather than simply de-risking.
  5. A heavy jobs week. June payrolls land Thursday (a day early), new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh speaks Wednesday from Sintra, and futures still price a 69% chance of no July cut.

The Big Picture

What the tape is pricing this morning is not the end of the AI trade, but its first genuine stress test. The trigger was specific and almost banal — Apple raising hardware prices 15 to 25 percent, an admission that the chips powering the AI build-out are getting more expensive faster than demand can absorb. That detail mattered because it gave a number to a fear that had been building all week: that AI capital spending has run far ahead of cash returns. Layer in reports that OpenAI is pushing its IPO into 2027, and investors suddenly questioned whether public markets can keep digesting an endless pipeline of AI listings. The result was a vicious feedback loop — Wall Street's Thursday wobble cascaded into Friday's Asian bloodbath, which dragged US and European futures lower, which fed back into Asia.

Yet the most revealing number is the one that didn't move much: the VIX actually fell 2.5% to 18.41. That is not the signature of a market in panic — it is the signature of a violent rotation. Money is leaving the most crowded, most leveraged corner of the market — Korean retail ETFs, semiconductor megacaps, crypto — and moving into gold and Treasuries, where the 10-year yield is down seven basis points on the week. The bull case is arithmetic: the KOSPI entered the week up 62% for the quarter and the Nikkei up 34%, so a double-digit pullback is mechanical profit-taking, not a thesis breaking. The bear case is that valuations this stretched need only an excuse, and a SpaceX share lock-up expiry in August now looms as the next supply shock. Both camps will be reading this morning's Tokyo and Seoul prints for the answer.

Markets

Equities. Wall Street's Friday was a study in exhaustion rather than recovery: the S&P 500 closed essentially flat at 7,354.02, the Dow shed 44.51 points to 51,876.11, and the Nasdaq Composite slipped 0.24% to 25,297.62 — its fifth straight loss. The stability was relative and fragile; it held only because the carnage had already been exported overnight to Asia, where SoftBank fell nearly 13% and Samsung and SK Hynix were dumped wholesale.

Rates & FX. Haven demand pulled the 10-year Treasury yield down about seven basis points on the week to roughly 4.37%, a notable move given May PCE printed hotter than hoped. The dollar index eased 0.1% to 101.34 and the euro held near $1.140 — a soft-dollar, bid-bond combination that says the market wants safety without betting on imminent Fed cuts.

Commodities & crypto. Brent dropped to around $72 and WTI to $68.86, both five-month lows, as vessels move openly through the Strait of Hormuz under a US-Iran thaw and Gulf exports rebuild toward pre-war levels. Gold pushed up 0.7% to $4,063. Bitcoin was the cleanest casualty of the risk purge, falling 3.7% to about $58,981 — roughly $48,000 below its level a year ago.

"The AI trade didn't break on Friday — it blinked. Whether Tokyo and Seoul call this a correction or a top is the only question that matters this morning."

The Fed Enters a Loaded Week

Into this volatility walks a jobs report and a new Fed chair. Kevin Warsh — installed this spring and openly preferring that central bankers say less in public — makes a rare appearance Wednesday at the ECB's Sintra symposium, where any hint about his plans for the rate path will be parsed line by line. The backdrop is awkward: President Trump wants cuts, last year's tariffs and this year's Gulf conflict are still nudging prices higher, and the funds rate sits at 3.50–3.75% after the June hold. Futures price a 69% probability the Fed stays put on July 29.

Thursday's June payrolls report, pulled forward a day because US markets close for Independence Day, is the week's hinge. Consensus sits at 172,000, matching May, but Barclays sees just 100,000 alongside a steady 4.3% jobless rate — a print that would still keep the Fed comfortably on hold while quietly flagging a labor market that ran hot in the second quarter after a soft first one. For a market suddenly nervous about whether AI capex can be sustained, a resilient jobs number is double-edged: it supports earnings, but removes the rate-cut cushion equity bulls have been leaning on.

The Bottom Line

Friday was not a crash — it was a deleveraging, and the calm VIX proves it. The AI thesis is intact, but its price was not, and the market spent the weekend deciding whether $72 oil and a soft-dollar haven bid are the start of a rotation or merely a pause. This morning's Asian tape is the first vote; Thursday's payrolls are the second. Until both are counted, treat every bounce as a question, not an answer.
