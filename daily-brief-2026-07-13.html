Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Iran-US strikes resume. The ceasefire collapsed; the US launched a new wave targeting Iranian military infrastructure across the Gulf — roughly 140 targets on Saturday, an Iranian counterstrike early Sunday — even as Trump signaled willingness to resume talks.
  2. The Fed enters July divided. Chair Kevin Warsh testifies Monday and Tuesday amid an internal "family fight" over tightening; futures price ~70% odds of a hold on July 29 but keep a bias toward 4% rates by year-end.
  3. Inflation data is the turning point. Monday's CPI (4:30 PM GST) and Tuesday's PPI carry outsized weight; consensus sees headline CPI at 3.8% YoY, down from 4.2% in May. Any surprise reframes the whole rate path.
  4. Equities held ground despite geopolitics. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq notched a third consecutive winning week; the VIX rose only modestly to 16.9 — measured anxiety, not panic flight.
  5. Asia opens mixed after the shock. The Nikkei gained 1.2% as the yen weakened on a safe-haven reversal; Shanghai fell 0.97% on renewed growth concerns; the Hang Seng held +0.45%.

The Big Picture

Friday's US close saw the major indices eke out gains — the S&P 500 +0.42% to 7,575, the Nasdaq +0.29%, the Dow +0.29% — completing a winning week despite escalating geopolitical alarm. The mid-June ceasefire between the US and Iran, meant as a gateway to 60-day peace talks, fractured on July 8 when Iran allegedly struck commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz and the US responded with airstrikes. Three rounds of tit-for-tat followed: US Central Command hit roughly 140 Iranian targets on Saturday, and Iran counterstruck early Sunday. The market's muted reaction — VIX at 16.9, up but nowhere near panic — says investors are pricing a contained conflict: no full war, both sides still signaling talks. Oil actually fell 1.2% to $79.10; gold slipped as safe-haven demand ebbed; equities held because earnings and Fed policy, not geopolitics, remain the tape's primary drivers.

The real fork in the road arrives this week. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testifies Monday and Tuesday, entering a "family fight" inside the central bank over whether to tighten, hold, or prepare markets for cuts. Futures price ~70% odds of a hold at the July 28–29 meeting, but June's dot plot shifted higher — pointing to at least one hike by year-end and rates near 4% through mid-2027. All of it hinges on inflation. Monday at 4:30 PM GST, June CPI lands: consensus 3.8% YoY, down from 4.2%, helped by a ~10% drop in gasoline prices. A downside surprise revives rate-cut odds; a hot print hardens "hold indefinitely" into "hike." These two days define risk appetite for the rest of July.

"The ceasefire is over. We're in talks." — Trump, July 12. The market now prices a contained conflict, not a conflagration — and hands the microphone back to the CPI.

Markets

Equities. All three major US indices closed positive Friday, with the Nasdaq and S&P 500 notching their third consecutive winning week. The Dow added 149.6 points (+0.29%) to 52,637, extending a rally that has carried it toward record territory despite Middle East turbulence. Asia opened mixed: Japan's Nikkei +1.2% to 68,557 on yen weakness as safe-haven positioning unwound, China's Shanghai Composite −0.97% on persistent growth concerns, Hong Kong's Hang Seng steady at +0.45% (24,052). The divergence reflects asymmetric exposure — Japanese exporters benefit from yen depreciation; Chinese equities face renewed slowdown fears. Europe closed Friday flat to slightly lower (Stoxx 600 at 641; Euro Stoxx 50 −0.3% to 6,267), a cautious footing ahead of Fed testimony and the inflation prints.

Rates & FX. The 10-year Treasury yield held at 4.57%, up roughly 5 basis points on the week as inflation expectations proved sticky. The dollar index eased to 100.7 (−0.3%) as risk sentiment stabilized and safe-haven demand ebbed; the euro firmed at the margin. The Fed's internal split — Warsh cautioning against further tightening while June's dot plot leans hawkish — argues for a July hold, but futures still sketch a drift to ~3.8% by October and 4% by year-end if inflation refuses to break. Every basis point on the 10-year feeds straight into equity valuations; this week's data resets that trajectory.

Commodities & crypto. Brent fell 1.2% to $79.10 — the paradox of the session, escalating Middle East tension against a muted geopolitical premium, weighed down by oversupply signals. Gold retreated 0.8% to $4,095 as dollar weakness and equity strength blunted its haven bid. Bitcoin rallied 1.5% to $62,847, a bet that the Iran crisis stays containable and that any inflation softness brings accommodation back into view. The VIX closed at 16.9, up 4.8% on the day but well below 20 — measured anxiety, volatility expected, panic absent.

The Fed "Family Fight" and Inflation's Final Word

Inside the Federal Reserve, an institutional battle has erupted over the path of policy. Chair Kevin Warsh, testifying before Congress on Monday and Tuesday, has signaled caution against further tightening. But the June Summary of Economic Projections shifted the median year-end rate forecast higher — pointing toward at least one hike, not a cut, and rates near 4% through mid-2027. Inflation, though decelerating, sits above the 2% target. The CME FedWatch tool prices a 70.1% probability of a hold at the July 28–29 meeting — with room for pivots if the data surprises.

The market has grafted its entire bull case onto the assumption that inflation is tamed and rates are set to peak. One strong CPI print — or even a stubborn hold at 3.8% — could fracture that consensus. If Monday's CPI comes in at consensus or below, expect rate expectations for Q4 to reprice lower and longer-dated Treasuries to rally. If it surprises hot (4.0%+), the "hold forever" narrative collapses, yields spike, and equities face a reassessment of discount rates. Warsh's tone is the swing factor: dovish rhetoric can offset a hot print; hawkish language can neutralize a soft one.

The Bottom Line

Markets have built an increasingly fragile bull case on the assumption that inflation is tamed and the Fed holds through 2026. Iran's escalation tests that thesis by re-introducing geopolitical risk — and the VIX at 16.9 with oil falling says investors are watching, not panicking. The real test arrives Monday: CPI at consensus 3.8% or below keeps the "hold" narrative intact and equities bid; a hot print reprices the entire rate path, spikes yields, and compresses valuations. Warsh's testimony is the swing voice on both days. Geopolitics matter, but this week central-bank policy and inflation data matter more. Watch the 4:30 PM GST print on Monday — everything else is noise until it lands.
