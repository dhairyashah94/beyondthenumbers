The Fed Flipped the Script — and the Dollar, Gold and Crypto Are All Repricing for a Hike

Wall Street drifted into the close as a hawkish Fed left every asset class hunting for a new equilibrium. This morning the dollar sits near a one-year high, gold has cracked $4,000, and a Micron blowout after the bell is the lone reminder that the AI boom hasn't paused.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The Fed's dot plot flipped to a hike. Futures now imply a roughly 77% chance of a rate increase by December, up from about 24% a month ago, with an October move all but fully priced.
  2. Gold cracked $4,000. Bullion fell 2.8% to around $3,999, its lowest since November 2025, as a stronger dollar and hawkish repricing drained the trade.
  3. Micron blew past guidance after the bell. The memory maker beat across every metric on record margins, and the stock jumped about 13% in late trade toward its 52-week high.
  4. Oil kept sliding. Brent slipped below $76 and WTI under $70 as easing Gulf tensions and heavier Strait of Hormuz traffic loosened the supply premium.
  5. All eyes on core PCE at 4:30 PM Dubai time. The Fed's preferred inflation gauge lands today alongside final Q1 GDP and jobless claims — the cleanest test yet of the hike narrative.

The Big Picture

For the first time this cycle, the market is no longer arguing about when the Federal Reserve cuts — it is pricing whether the Fed must hike. Last week's FOMC held the target range at 3.50%–3.75%, but the projections did the talking: the median policymaker now sees rates ending 2026 higher than today, a clean reversal from March, and 17 of 18 officials judge inflation risks to be tilted to the upside. The Fed's own forecast for headline PCE jumped to 3.6% for year-end versus 2.7% previously. That is not a pause that refreshes; it is a pause that warns.

The repricing is showing up everywhere at once, which is what makes this more than a rates story. A dollar near its strongest in a year is the transmission belt: it is what dragged gold below $4,000, pressured crypto toward two-week lows, and capped the dollar-denominated commodity complex even as oil fell for its own geopolitical reasons. Equities, notably, are the asset refusing to panic — the S&P 500 shed just 0.10% and the Dow actually rose — because higher-for-longer is survivable when earnings are still compounding. That tension between a hawkish curve and resilient profits is the trade of the summer, and today's inflation print will tug hard on one side of it.

Markets

Equities: muted, not fearful. Wall Street closed mixed: the Dow added 182 points (+0.35%) to 51,848.90 while the Nasdaq slipped 0.43% to 25,476.64 and the S&P 500 eased 0.10% to 7,358.22, with chip names heavy into Micron's print. The VIX fell to 18.63 — a market unbothered rather than complacent. In Europe the Stoxx 600 closed down 0.7% at 634.63; across Asia, Tokyo's Nikkei finished Wednesday off 0.88% near 69,175 and Hong Kong's Hang Seng dropped 1.8% to 23,336, though this morning Micron's after-hours surge is lifting semiconductor sentiment.

Rates & FX: the dollar does the work. The 10-year Treasury yield eased to about 4.49% as progress toward a durable Gulf de-escalation trimmed the risk premium, even as the short end stays pinned by hike pricing near 4.15% on the 2-year. The dollar index firmed 0.28% to 101.64, hovering at a one-year high; EUR/USD slipped to roughly 1.135, touching 1.1361 overnight as the rate-differential trade ran back in the greenback's favor.

Commodities & crypto: the strong-dollar casualties. Gold was the headline loser, down 2.8% to around $3,999 and through $4,000 for the first time since November. Crude extended its slide — Brent below $76 (−1.7%) and WTI under $70 — on easing Hormuz risk. Bitcoin held near $62,651, up 0.6% on the day but still pinned at a two-week low as the strong dollar siphons speculative flows.

The Hawkish Repricing

The speed of the move is the story. A month ago, fed-funds futures gave a December hike barely a one-in-four chance; today that probability sits near 77%, and an October increase is essentially fully discounted. Behind the shift is a Fed that has stopped treating sticky inflation as transitory — core PCE is now projected to accelerate to 3.3% by year-end, and the committee's near-unanimous tilt toward upside risk hands policy a hawkish bias it lacked just one meeting ago. The bond market has obliged, lifting the front end and steepening the conversation around terminal rates.

What today's data could do is settle the argument. A hot May core PCE would validate the dot plot and likely extend the dollar's run while pressuring gold and rate-sensitive equities further; a soft print would be the first crack in the hike thesis and could spark a sharp relief rally in everything the strong dollar has bruised. With the AI complex — underscored by Micron's record quarter — still supplying the bull case, the market is being asked to hold two ideas at once: profits up, policy tighter.

The Bottom Line

This is no longer a market waiting for relief — it is one bracing for tightening. The dollar at a one-year high and gold through $4,000 are the bond market's hike bet bleeding into everything else, and only resilient earnings are keeping equities upright. Today's core PCE will tell you which conviction wins: if inflation stays hot, the hawks own the summer; if it cools, the most violent rally will be in whatever the strong dollar has punished hardest.
