A 57,000-Job Miss Silences the Fed's Hawks — and Splits the Market in Two

Overnight on Wall Street, the weakest US payrolls print in a year sent the Dow to a record while the AI-chip complex cracked for a second day. This morning in Seoul, the Kospi is clawing back Thursday's 7.9% plunge. US markets are shut today for Independence Day.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Payrolls stumbled. The US added just 57,000 jobs in June against a 115,000 consensus, with April and May revised down by a combined 74,000 — and unemployment fell to 4.2% only because participation slumped to 61.5%, its lowest since March 2021.
  2. The Dow hit a record as chips cracked. The Dow jumped 594.83 points (+1.14%) to 52,900.07 while the Nasdaq fell 0.8%; the VanEck Semiconductor ETF sank 4.5%, led by Micron's 10.6% slide.
  3. Asia is rebounding this morning. Seoul's Kospi is up 4.65% after Thursday's 7.9% collapse in SK Hynix and Samsung; the Nikkei has added 1.36% and the Hang Seng 1.57% in afternoon trade.
  4. Warsh cooled the hike talk. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh said "inflation risks have come down" and promised a "good family fight" at the July 28–29 FOMC; futures still price the funds rate near 4% by year-end, versus 3.50–3.75% today.
  5. Oil is back at pre-war levels. Brent slid 1.4% toward $70 as Strait of Hormuz flows recover and US–Iran talks in Qatar make progress; Wall Street is closed today, reopening Monday at 5:30 PM GST.

The Big Picture

The June jobs report was weak in the way that matters most: not one bad month, but a trend confirmed. A 57,000 print against the 12-month average of 36,000 hardly screams recession — yet the downward revisions to April and May, and a participation rate now at a five-year low, describe a labor market that is shrinking its way to a lower unemployment rate. That distinction is what the market priced within minutes: the two-year yield fell 3.5 basis points to 4.13% and traders pared bets that the Fed's next move — a hike, in this cycle — comes as soon as September. With CPI running at 4.2% in May, the Fed's problem has been too much inflation, not too little growth. Thursday's data suggests the economy may be solving the demand side of that problem on its own.

The second story is the one splitting the tape. The Dow's 594-point record close and the Nasdaq's decline are not contradictory; they are the same trade viewed from both ends. Money is rotating out of the AI-hardware complex — where Meta's plan to sell excess computing capacity and reports that Apple is testing Chinese memory suppliers have raised the specter of oversupply — and into financials, industrials and other rate-sensitive value. Seoul felt it worst: Thursday's 7.9% Kospi plunge, driven by a 14.6% drop in SK Hynix, was the sharpest expression yet of the market's question about who actually gets paid for the trillions in AI capex. This morning's 4.65% Kospi rebound says dip-buyers still exist. It does not say the question is answered.

Markets

Equities. The S&P 500 closed effectively flat at 7,483.24 — a record-adjacent standstill masking violent internals. Micron fell 10.6%, SanDisk 14% and Marvell 9.8%, while Meta jumped 8.8% on its compute-leasing pivot and Reddit gained 13.9%. Tesla slipped despite delivering 480,126 vehicles in Q2, well above the 406,600 expected — a beat sold, which tells you where sentiment sits. In Europe, the Stoxx 600 added 0.1% Thursday to hold near 640 after June eurozone inflation undershot at 2.8% (consensus 3.0%), easing pressure on the ECB. This morning's Gulf sessions face a quiet, US-holiday tape into the weekend.

Rates & FX. The 10-year Treasury yield eased to 4.47% after testing 4.5%, and the policy-sensitive 2-year fell to 4.13% as hike bets were trimmed. The real bond story is Tokyo: the 10-year JGB reached 2.77% and the 30-year closed above 4.00% for the first time since May, keeping the yen pinned near 162.35 per dollar — a whisker from its post-1980s low of 162.84. The dollar index slipped 0.3% to 101.1, and sterling closed below 0.86 per euro for the first time in a year.

Commodities & crypto. Brent's third straight decline, to $70.60, completes the round trip to pre-war levels as Hormuz flows normalize and SPR releases continue — though refined-product tightness is keeping pump prices sticky. Gold held near $4,080 after briefly topping $4,100 Wednesday, supported by a one-year inflation swap that has collapsed from 3.5% in May to about 2.1%. Bitcoin surged roughly 6% to the $61,000–62,000 area, bouncing off quarterly lows after spot ETFs bled a record $4.5 billion in June — a 20.5% monthly drawdown that stalled crypto legislation has done nothing to repair.

"Unemployment fell for the wrong reason: fewer Americans are looking for work at all. That is not a labor market the Fed needs to cool — it is one it needs to understand."

The Warsh Fed: A Hike Still Priced, a Fight Still Coming

Kevin Warsh has spent his first months as Fed Chair dismantling forward guidance, telling investors to "look to the data, not the Fed." The data are now doing the talking. The June dot plot's median called for the funds rate to end 2026 at 3.8% — at least one hike from today's 3.50–3.75% range — with nine of 18 officials above the current range. After Thursday's payrolls, futures still trace a path to roughly 3.8% by October and near 4% at year-end, holding through mid-2027. In other words, the market has trimmed the timing, not the destination. Warsh's own signal — inflation risks "have come down" alongside energy prices, and a promised "good family fight" at the July 28–29 meeting — suggests a chair inclined to let restrictive-enough ride while the committee's hawks argue for insurance against a CPI still above 4%.

The tension for the second half is this: if participation keeps falling, wage pressure can persist even as hiring stalls — supply-side inflation the Fed cannot hike away. Thursday's report cut the probability of a September move without resolving whether the last mile of disinflation arrives via a softer economy or a smaller workforce. The July FOMC now matters more than the June one did.

The Bottom Line

The market spent Thursday telling you two things at once: the Fed's hiking case is wounded, and the AI trade's margin of error is gone. A 57,000-job economy with 4%-plus inflation is not a market that rewards owning everything — it rewards knowing which half of the tape you are standing in. Enjoy the quiet US holiday; the July 28–29 FOMC "family fight" and Q2 earnings will not stay quiet for long.
