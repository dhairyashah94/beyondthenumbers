Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Iran ceasefire over. Trump declared an end to July's understanding with Iran overnight after renewed military strikes, reigniting geopolitical risk and energy volatility across the Gulf.
  2. Oil surges on conflict risk. Brent jumped 1.4% to $79.25/bbl and WTI opened near $72, threatening to rekindle the inflation pressure the Fed has been watching closely.
  3. Stocks recover despite the headlines. Overnight US markets rallied on chip-sector strength; the Nasdaq outperformed at +1.30%, a sign that liquidity trumped geopolitical concern.
  4. Treasury yields climb on inflation bets. The 10-year yield rose to 4.58%, reflecting expectations that energy shocks will keep the Fed patient on rate cuts through year-end.
  5. Asia-Pacific mixed overnight. Japan's Nikkei gained 1.38% and Shanghai +1.65%, but Hong Kong's Hang Seng slipped 0.70% as energy-import concerns weighed.

The Big Picture

The market's overnight arc — a dip on the ceasefire collapse, then a recovery on chip strength — captures the current paradox: geopolitical shocks are now less powerful than sector rotation. The S&P 500's 0.81% gain and the Nasdaq's 1.30% jump suggest traders are pricing a scenario in which the US can sustain elevated oil into Q3 without derailing corporate earnings. Energy equities, often the first casualty when oil rises on supply-shock fears, were notably contained. The dollar held near 101 and gold pushed to $4,110 on late safe-haven demand — a market bracing, but not panicking.

But the real risk is inflation. Brent's 1.4% jump and the 10-year yield's climb to 4.58% show markets are no longer indifferent to $80 oil. A sustained price spike would force the Fed's hand: traders currently price about 74.9% odds of a hold at the July 29 FOMC, with a 25.1% chance of a 25-basis-point hike. The risk to the script is clear — if energy stays elevated and wage growth stays sticky, the Fed loses room to ease as aggressively as some have penciled in for 2027.

Markets

Equities. Semiconductors were the session's heroes. SK Hynix, Nvidia and TSMC-linked names surged overnight, lifting the Nasdaq +1.30% to 26,206.89 and dragging the S&P 500 up +0.81% to 7,543.64. The Dow's modest +0.27% to 52,487.41 reflects sector concentration: financials and industrials, more sensitive to energy costs and geopolitical hedging, lagged. Asia mirrored the chip bet — Japan's Nikkei rose 1.38% to 67,743.85 and Shanghai +1.65% to 4,036.59 on export optimism — while Hong Kong's Hang Seng dipped 0.70% to 24,030.18 as energy-import concerns weighed.

Rates & FX. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed to 4.58% from 4.56%, a one-basis-point rise that nonetheless signals conviction that energy shocks will keep the Fed on hold. The dollar index held near 101, underpinned by safe-haven flows and rate differentials; the euro edged up to 1.1438 (+0.18%), rebounding from June lows as traders price another ECB move in September. Fed futures now price ~74.9% odds of a hold on July 29, with 25.1% tilted toward a 25bp hike — a material shift from the dovish pricing of early June.

Commodities & crypto. Brent surged $1.08 to $79.25/bbl (+1.38%) and WTI opened near $72.22 as the ceasefire collapse spooked traders on Strait of Hormuz risk. Gold, a typical beneficiary of geopolitical tension, was mixed — August futures opened $4,087.60 and rallied to $4,110.60 by mid-morning ET, a sign of late-hour safe-haven demand. Bitcoin held $63,257.52, reflecting a muted crypto reaction to the headline risk. The oil move is the session's pivot: a sustained push above $80 Brent would materially alter both Fed calculus and corporate margin assumptions.

"If oil holds north of $79, inflation stays sticky and the Fed's rate-cut window slams shut. Markets just got a reminder that geopolitical shocks can still matter."

The Energy Shock Calculus

On July 9, President Trump declared an end to the month-old ceasefire with Iran after a fresh volley of military strikes. The market's first reaction was brutal — oil surged and equities sold off hard. But by the overnight US close, equities had recovered on chip strength, a classic risk-on reversion. The question is whether that reversion holds if oil stays elevated.

For the Fed, the arithmetic is unforgiving. Inflation remains above target, wage growth is sticky, and energy shocks are material again. The consensus view — a hold on July 29 and gradual easing through 2027 — assumes no major supply disruption. But if Brent stays above $75 and creeps toward $85, core inflation expectations will shift upward and the Fed's room to cut in September or November evaporates. Traders are hedging already: the 10-year yield rose on the day even as equities recovered, a classic "yields rise on inflation risk" signal. Watch today's energy complex and any Strait of Hormuz headlines; they will frame the Fed's next move more than earnings surprises.

The Bottom Line

The overnight rally on chip strength masked a hard truth: energy markets are live again, and geopolitical shocks matter for inflation. Brent at $79.25 is not a crisis yet, but $85+ would fundamentally alter Fed expectations and corporate earnings math. Traders just repriced 25 basis points of hike risk into the July 29 odds; if the Iran situation escalates or proves sustained, the market will reprice the entire 2026–2027 rate path. Today's focus: watch crude, watch earnings commentary, watch central-bank rhetoric. The narrative has shifted from "easy cuts in 2027" to "patient hold, maybe a hike." Energy just rewrote the script.
