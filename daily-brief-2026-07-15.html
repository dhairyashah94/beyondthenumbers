June CPI landed at 3.5% — below consensus — and the odds of a July rate hike collapsed from 42% to 17% in a matter of hours. The relief came with a sting: growth stocks sold off hard as investors rotated to value, oil surged on Middle East supply fears, and Asia opened in a broad relief rally. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh takes the Senate stand at 6:00 PM GST.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. Inflation damps Fed rate bets. June CPI printed 3.5% year-over-year — below the 3.8% consensus and the lowest reading in 18 months. July rate-hike odds crashed from 42% to 17% within hours, and bond markets now price a growing chance the Fed holds through year-end.
  2. Tech bore the brunt. The Nasdaq dropped 1.6% against the S&P 500's 0.8% decline as investors rotated from growth to value — broad profit-taking in megacap software and cloud that semiconductor strength couldn't offset.
  3. Warsh's Senate debut is today. New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testifies before the Senate Banking Committee at 6:00 PM GST — his first formal congressional appearance since taking office. Yesterday he declared the inflation surge "will be a thing of the past."
  4. Oil surges on geopolitical heat. Brent climbed 4.5% to $86.82/bbl as the Hormuz blockade and supply fears overrode any demand-side softness — a 10% weekly gain that is now testing conviction on both sides of the trade.
  5. Asia shakes off Wall Street's gloom. Nikkei +0.9%, Hang Seng +0.8%, and a stunning +6.3% on the Kospi — a sharp relief rally led by small-caps and healthcare on expectations the Fed's pivot lightens Asia's carry-trade pressures.

The Big Picture

Markets are repricing the inflation endgame. Yesterday's June CPI of 3.5% year-over-year — the lowest print in 18 months, and a cumulative five-point fall from the 8.5% peak of 2022 — forced a tectonic shift in rate expectations. The odds of a July Fed funds increase evaporated from 42% to 17% in a matter of hours, and bond markets priced in a growing chance that the central bank holds through year-end. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's remarks amplified the data: his declaration that "the inflation surge of the last five years will be a thing of the past" moved markets more than the percentage point of price data alone.

But the relief came with a sharp sting. Growth stocks — the darlings of a low-rate regime — sold off hard. The Nasdaq fell 1.6%, underperforming the S&P 500's 0.8% decline, as investors rotated into value and cyclicals. Treasury yields fell roughly 5 basis points across the curve. Commodity markets told a different story entirely: Brent surged 4.5% to test $87 as Middle East tensions and tight supply overrode macro softness, and gold rose 2% to $4,080 as real yields compressed. The divergence is the story of the morning — financial conditions are loosening while energy and real-asset trades are tightening.

The Inflation Pivot and What It Means for Asset Prices

Yesterday's print didn't just move markets — it reframed the entire macro narrative for the second half of 2026. A 3.5% June CPI lands below the 3.8% consensus and marks the first meaningful confirmation that the stagflationary fears which gripped markets in late 2025 are giving way to disinflation expectations and rate-cut speculation. Wage pressures remain elevated, but the trajectory is unmistakable.

The immediate casualty was the growth–value spread. Nasdaq futures had priced in a July hold; overnight they repriced the entire cycle. CME FedWatch odds of a July hike fell from 42% to 17%, September hike odds dropped from 55% to 35%, and December cut odds widened from 25% to 40%. That dynamic inverts the investment landscape: lower-duration assets — long-dated Treasuries, low-volatility equities — rally, while high-growth, high-duration names sell. The 1.6% Nasdaq decline is the market's reallocation signal, not a recession warning. Commodity bulls read the same data differently: weaker growth expectations could depress demand, yet Brent printed a 4.5% rally to $86.82. That spread between soft data and strong oil reflects supply dynamics and geopolitical premiums overriding macro softness — the blockade premium is now embedded in the curve.

Markets

Equities: rotation, not recession. The S&P 500's 0.8% overnight decline masks a brutal sector divergence. Tech and growth took the full hit while financials and energy rallied on lower rate expectations and higher oil. This is classic risk-on behavior dressed as risk-off: yields fell, real rates compressed, and the market rewarded cyclicals and value. Breadth was negative, but that is consistent with portfolio rebalancing, not capitulation. Asia's open — Nikkei +0.9%, Hang Seng +0.8%, Kospi +6.3% — suggests the region reads the Fed's pivot as relief: lighter carry-trade pressure and cheaper dollar funding ahead. The Kospi's surge, led by small-caps and healthcare, is the most emphatic vote of confidence in the easing narrative anywhere in the complex this morning.

Rates & FX: the long-duration play. Treasury yields fell roughly 5 basis points across the curve, with the 10-year touching fresh multi-month lows near 3.9% — the move most violent in the 20- and 30-year tenors as long-duration bonds rallied hard. The dollar index eased 0.5% to 100.91 as carry-trade bets unwound; a lower Fed funds path makes dollar funding cheaper for leveraged positions. EUR/USD held near $1.14, consistent with ECB divergence — Frankfurt is also debating cuts but moving slower — while the pound weakened on BoE fade talk. This is a classic "lower for longer" trade: real rates down, nominal yields down, dollar softer, havens bid.

Commodities & crypto: oil's Middle East premium. Crude surged despite soft demand signals — Brent up 4.5% to $86.82, a 10% weekly gain that tests conviction, with WTI lagging but following. Supply fears and the Hormuz standoff continue to override macro softness. Gold climbed 2% to $4,080 as real yields fell and hedging appetite returned. Bitcoin slid 2.3% to $62,550, weighed by the derating of growth names — crypto's muted response to the inflation print suggests the asset class is increasingly correlated to equity technicals rather than macro rate expectations. The divergence is instructive: commodities are trading supply and geopolitics; digital assets are following equity momentum.

The Bottom Line

Markets are now pricing an inflation victory for the Federal Reserve and a pivot toward easing by late 2026 or early 2027. Growth stocks paid the price for yesterday's relief — but this is a reallocation, not a bear market. Energy and cyclicals are the beneficiaries, supported by geopolitical supply fears and real-yield compression; Asia's strength says the region sees carry-trade risk receding. Today's PPI print and Warsh's Senate testimony will either confirm the new narrative or inject doubt — expect volatility if either surprises hawkish. For now the trade is lower rates, higher energy, and a tactical shift from momentum to value. That trade is already crowded, which makes it vulnerable to data reversals — execution matters more than conviction in the days ahead.
