Are Global Equity Markets Disconnected from the Real Economy?

Stocks just closed a third straight winning week while a war burns in the Gulf and the IMF marks global growth down. Market cap is at twice the dot-com peak relative to GDP, ten companies are 40% of the index, and one capex cycle is carrying most of US growth. Time to put the market and the economy on the same page — and see which one is lying.

By Dhairya Shah · 14 min read · A Behind The Numbers big read · Data as of July 10–13, 2026

The Answer in Five Lines

  1. The valuation gap is at a record. US equity market capitalization stands near 218% of GDP (Q1 2026, Federal Reserve data) — about two standard deviations above trend. The dot-com peak was 146%. The Shiller CAPE ratio has touched 40, a level previously seen only in the tech bubble.
  2. Growth does not explain it. The IMF projects global growth of 3.0–3.1% in 2026 — below the 3.4–3.5% pace of 2024–25 and well under the 3.7% average of 2000–19 — while wartime inflation is rising, not falling.
  3. Concentration, not breadth, drives the gap. The top 10 S&P 500 firms are roughly 40% of index value (26.6% at the 2000 peak) but only about a third of index earnings. Since 2019, US market cap has grown roughly 2.7 times faster than nominal GDP.
  4. The market and the economy now share one engine. AI-related investment contributed roughly 74% of US GDP growth in Q1 2026; hyperscaler capex is set to nearly double to $630–750 billion this year — approaching 5% of US GDP, a share last seen in the late-1990s telecom boom.
  5. The exposure is asymmetric. The top 1% of US households hold 50% of corporate equities; the top 10% hold 87%; the bottom half hold 1%. A correction would transmit to the economy through a narrow, wealthy consumption channel — and through the capex cycle itself.

1 · Records on Wall Street, markdowns everywhere else

The question of whether stock markets track the real economy is as old as index investing, and the standard answer — markets lead, economies follow, and over long horizons the two reconcile — has survived most cycles. It is being asked again in 2026 with unusual urgency, because the two series it compares have rarely pointed in such different directions. Equity markets have completed three consecutive winning weeks through mid-July even as a shooting war between the United States and Iran disrupts Gulf shipping; the S&P 500 sits near record highs and its aggregate market capitalization reached a record $67 trillion in the second quarter. The real economy, meanwhile, is being marked down: the IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook — subtitled, without irony, Global Economy in the Shadow of War — cut its reference forecast for global growth to 3.1% for 2026, and its July update trims that to 3.0%, against a pre-pandemic (2000–19) average of 3.7%. Global headline inflation is projected to rise to 4.7% this year on energy and food prices before easing in 2027.

Slowing growth, rising inflation, elevated public debt, an active regional war — and record equity prices. Either the market is seeing something the growth data can't, or it has quietly stopped pricing the broad economy altogether. So let's do what this newsletter exists to do: put the two series on the same page and look. First the gap itself. Then where it lives — because it has a very specific address. Then the strongest case that nothing is wrong. And finally the uncomfortable part: why, this cycle, Main Street has more riding on Wall Street than the other way around.

2 · The gap you can't explain away

The broadest single gauge of market size relative to economic activity is the ratio of equity market capitalization to GDP — the "Buffett indicator." On Federal Reserve financial-accounts data, the US ratio stood at 218% of GDP after the Q1 2026 GDP estimate, roughly 57% above its long-run trend and about two standard deviations from it; intraday trackers computing the ratio on live market values put it near 237–241% in the second week of July, an all-time high. For scale: the ratio hovered between roughly 40% and 100% for the four decades before the late 1990s, peaked at 146% in the dot-com bubble, and reached about 109% before the 2008 crisis. Every previous episode at two standard deviations above trend — the late 1960s, 2000, 2021 — was followed by a drawdown of 25% or more. Valuation ratios based on earnings tell the same story: the S&P 500 trades near 31 times trailing earnings, and the Shiller cyclically-adjusted P/E touched 40 in January 2026, a reading matched only in the tech bubble in a century and a half of data.

Set that against output. Between end-2019 and mid-2026, US nominal GDP grew roughly 47% (from $21.7 trillion to about $31.9 trillion). Total US market capitalization grew approximately 126% over the same window (from about $34 trillion to $76.8 trillion). After-tax corporate profits — the fundamental that should arbitrate between the two — grew roughly 60–65% on BEA data: much faster than GDP, much slower than market value. In other words, even after crediting corporate America with a genuinely extraordinary profit expansion, valuation multiples, not fundamentals, account for a large share of the post-pandemic rally. The market has re-rated, not merely re-earned.

The global picture is more moderate but directionally identical. World listed market capitalization is estimated near $154 trillion in 2026 against world output of roughly $115–120 trillion — a global ratio in the vicinity of 130%, historically elevated — with the United States alone accounting for about half of world market value. Global growth, meanwhile, is slowing and its composition worsening: the IMF's July update projects 3.0% for 2026 with headline inflation rising to 4.7%, and its April scenarios put growth as low as 2.0–2.5% if the Middle East conflict broadens. The Fund's own list of top downside risks now includes, explicitly, "a reassessment of expectations surrounding artificial-intelligence-driven productivity" — the central bank of the world economy's way of saying that the market's key assumption is itself a systemic risk factor.

3 · The disconnect has an address

Aggregate ratios obscure the mechanism. The re-rating documented above is not spread across thousands of listed firms; it is concentrated to a degree without precedent in modern index history. The ten largest S&P 500 companies now represent roughly 40% of index market value — J.P. Morgan puts the figure at 40.8% as of May, some trackers above 42% — versus a range of 18–23% that held from 1990 to 2015 and a dot-com peak of 26.6%. Nvidia alone carries a weight near 7%, larger than entire sectors. Those same ten firms, however, generate only about one-third of index earnings, and the gap between weight and earnings contribution has widened materially since 2015, when the two were roughly aligned. The equal-weighted S&P 500 — identical companies, equal slices — has lagged the cap-weighted index persistently since 2023, and the cap-weighted index now trades at a premium of nearly 30% to its equal-weighted twin, up from 13% pre-pandemic.

What binds the top of the index together is a single investment thesis. The market's leadership is either building artificial-intelligence infrastructure, supplying it, or expected to monetize it. That thesis is now visible in the national accounts, not just in stock prices: the five largest US hyperscalers have committed roughly $630–690 billion of capital expenditure for 2026 — with estimates including the wider data-center complex approaching $750 billion — nearly double 2025 levels, and Goldman Sachs' baseline model projects the build-out compounding to $1.6 trillion annually by 2031. In the first quarter of 2026, computer and peripheral equipment investment grew at a 67% annualized rate, and analysts attribute roughly 74% of the quarter's 2.1% annualized GDP growth to AI-related investment — more than consumer spending, the traditional engine of two-thirds of the US economy, contributed in total. AI-related capital formation is estimated near 5% of US GDP, the highest share for a single technology cycle since the late-1990s telecom build-out.

"The index is no longer a mirror of the economy. It is a leveraged bet on one technology transition — and the economy itself has started making the same bet."

4 · The best case that nothing is wrong

Three counter-arguments deserve honest weight — and if you post this piece on LinkedIn, you will hear all three within the hour. First, the leaders earn their weight — partly. Unlike the dot-com cohort, today's mega-caps are extraordinarily profitable: the ten largest S&P firms generate about a third of index profits, and by one estimate the "Magnificent Seven" account for roughly 70% of the index's economic profit. Alphabet alone earns about $160 billion a year, the most of any company on earth. A market that concentrates value in its most profitable firms is doing something rational, even if the degree is debatable. Second, listed markets are not the domestic economy. Roughly 40% of S&P 500 revenue is earned abroad, so comparing US market value to US GDP overstates the gap; globalization-adjusted versions of the Buffett indicator sit far lower (one such adjustment puts it near 114%). Large listed firms are also systematically more profitable, more global and more capital-light than the millions of private businesses GDP counts, and their share of total economic activity has grown for structural reasons — index composition, buybacks, the migration of value from labor to capital — that are not, strictly, mispricing. Third, markets price the future, not the present. A slow 2026 with a war premium is precisely the environment in which equities should look expensive on current output if investors genuinely expect AI to raise productivity growth. On that view the valuation gap is a forecast, not a failure.

Each argument narrows the indictment; none acquits. Profitability justifies concentration of earnings, but the market has concentrated value roughly ten percentage points faster than earnings. Foreign revenue has been rising for decades and cannot explain why the ratio doubled relative to its own 2007 level. And the "forecast" defense is exactly what the IMF now lists among its principal global risks: if AI-driven productivity disappoints, the forecast embedded in prices — trillions of dollars of it — gets marked down against an economy growing 3%. The disconnect thesis survives its best objections in weakened but recognizable form: prices have not left the economy behind entirely; they have left the measured, present-tense, median economy behind, in favor of a projected one.

5 · When the market becomes the economy

The classical worry about a market-economy gap is that Wall Street falls and Main Street barely notices — as in 1987, when a 20%+ single-day crash left GDP essentially untouched. The 2026 configuration is different in two ways that make the gap itself a macro-financial vulnerability. The first channel is the wealth effect, concentrated as never before. Federal Reserve distributional accounts show the top 1% of US households holding 50% of corporate equities (about $27.6 trillion as of Q1 2026), the top 10% holding 87%, and the bottom half of the country holding roughly 1%. High-income households, their balance sheets swollen by three years of equity gains, have been carrying US consumption while lower-income cohorts retrench. A serious correction would not democratically trim everyone's wealth; it would switch off the specific consumption engine the expansion currently runs on.

The second channel is the capex cycle itself. Because AI investment now contributes most of measured US growth, the stock market's central assumption — that the build-out continues and pays off — has become a GDP assumption. If equity prices reprice the AI thesis downward, hyperscaler boards do not need a recession to cut $100–200 billion of investment plans; a change of sentiment suffices, and that cut alone would remove more from GDP growth than any plausible consumer wobble. This is the sense in which the disconnection question inverts: the danger is no longer that the market ignores the economy, but that the economy has quietly hitched itself to the market's favorite story. The IMF's inclusion of AI-expectations risk alongside war and fragmentation in its global risk list acknowledges precisely this loop. Add the fiscal channel — capital-gains-sensitive tax revenue, elevated public debt, thinner buffers — and the cost of being wrong is not confined to portfolios.

Flashback · 2020, the dress rehearsal

The cleanest natural experiment on record: in 2020, global output contracted about 3.1% — the deepest peacetime recession since the 1930s — while the S&P 500 returned roughly +16% and world equity indices ended the year solidly higher. The mechanisms were policy liquidity, collapsed discount rates, and index composition tilted toward digital winners. The episode proved that a full calendar-year divergence between markets and measured output is possible and can be rational ex post. It also set the template now being repeated at larger scale: each time the divergence resolves in the market's favor, the incentive to fade the next one weakens — which is how gaps compound.

The bottom line

Are global equity markets disconnected from the real economy? After all the charts, the honest answer is: disconnected from the broad economy, fused to a narrow one. Aggregate valuations stand further from measured output than at any point in modern financial history, and slowing, war-shadowed global growth cannot close the gap arithmetically. But the gap is not a diffuse mania; it is a concentrated wager. A dozen firms, one technology thesis, and a capital-expenditure cycle worth roughly 5% of US GDP connect today's prices to tomorrow's hoped-for productivity. If the wager pays, the economy grows into its market and the "disconnect" retroactively becomes foresight, as it did after 2020. If it disappoints — the IMF's own listed risk — the correction would propagate through the most concentrated ownership structure on record and through the one investment engine currently generating growth.

For policymakers, the implication is uncomfortable: financial-stability risk now runs through equity concentration, not bank leverage. For investors, the actionable finding is narrower and older: the S&P 500 is no longer a diversified claim on the economy — it is approximately a 40% position in ten companies. And for anyone using the market as a barometer of national prosperity, the distributional data is decisive — 87% of the barometer belongs to a tenth of the households. The market is not lying about the economy. It is simply answering a different question: not "how is the economy doing?" but "who will own the next one?"
