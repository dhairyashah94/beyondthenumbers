Markets Hold Their Breath as the Fed Weighs a September Pivot

Equities drift near highs while the bond market quietly prices three cuts by year-end. The gap between those two views is where the risk lives.

By Dhairya Shah · 6 min read

There is a particular kind of quiet that settles over markets in the week before a Federal Reserve meeting, and this one is quieter than most. The S&P 500 has moved less than half a percent in either direction for five straight sessions. Volatility is asleep. And yet underneath the surface, the bond market has been doing a great deal of talking.

Fed funds futures now price roughly 70 basis points of easing by December — three cuts, give or take a coin flip. Two months ago that number was one cut, grudgingly. Nothing in the Fed's official communication has changed over that period. What changed is the data: two soft payroll prints, a core PCE reading that finally started with a two, and a consumer that is still spending but no longer surprising anyone.

What the bond market sees

The 2-year yield, the purest read on Fed expectations, has fallen 38 basis points since early April. That is not a panic move — it is a steady, deliberate repricing, the kind that happens when institutional money changes its mind slowly and then all at once. The curve has re-steepened to its widest level of the cycle, which historically happens for one of two reasons: the market expects cuts into a soft landing, or it expects cuts into something worse.

"Equities are priced for the first scenario. Bonds are hedging the second. Both cannot be fully right."

Equities, for their part, are priced for the benign version. The index trades at 21.5x forward earnings, with the median analyst still penciling in double-digit earnings growth for next year. That is a forecast that leaves very little room for the labor market to be telling us something real.

For Indian investors, the more interesting question is second-order. A Fed that cuts into a soft landing is historically the best possible backdrop for emerging-market flows — a weaker dollar, cheaper global funding, and risk appetite intact. The rupee has already started to behave as if that outcome is likely, and FII flows turned positive in May for the first time in four months.

The bottom line

Positioning for a pivot is no longer a contrarian trade — it is the consensus. That doesn't make it wrong. But when everyone is leaning the same way, the asymmetry moves to the other side of the boat. Wednesday's CPI print matters more than Thursday's meeting: a hot number doesn't just delay a cut, it breaks the story the whole market is currently telling itself. Stay long, stay hedged, and respect the tape.
