Running to Stand Still: Inside TCS's 14% Quarter

India's largest IT company opened earnings season with a headline that looked like a comeback — revenue up 14%. Look closer. A weak rupee and a soft year-ago base did most of the lifting, the operating margin slipped to 24%, and net profit rose just 5% after a legal charge. The one line growing fast is artificial intelligence. This is the quarter of a labour-arbitrage machine trying to reinvent itself as an AI shop before the old engine stalls.

The Quarter in Five Lines

  1. The 14% is real, but it isn't demand. Revenue in rupees jumped 14% year-on-year to ₹72,275 crore — largely because the rupee weakened and the June-2025 quarter was unusually soft. In constant currency the underlying pace was a fraction of that.
  2. Profit grew far slower than sales. Net profit rose only about 5% to ₹13,420 crore, weighed down by a ₹668 crore charge to settle a decade-old legal claim. The gap between 14% revenue and 5% profit is the story.
  3. Margins went the wrong way. The operating margin eased to 24.0%, down from 24.5% a year ago and below the 25% at which TCS exited FY26 — wage hikes and pricing pressure biting into the model.
  4. The order book held; AI accelerated. New deal wins (TCV) were a steady $9.5 billion, but annualised AI revenue hit $2.6 billion, up 13.6% in one quarter — anchored by an $800 million AI-led transformation deal with Sweden's SKF.
  5. The headcount is shrinking. The workforce fell to 593,798 as attrition stayed contained at 13.6%. A services giant that grows revenue while cutting people is telling you where it thinks the next margin dollar comes from.

1 · The headline that flatters

Every earnings season opens the same way in Mumbai: TCS reports first, and the market treats its number as the weather forecast for the whole IT sector. This quarter the forecast looked sunny — revenue from operations of ₹72,275 crore, up a striking 14% on the same quarter a year earlier. After two years of grinding, low-single-digit growth, a 14-handle reads like a turn in the cycle. It isn't. It is an exchange-rate story wearing a growth-story costume.

Two things inflated the headline. The first is the rupee: it weakened materially against the dollar over the year, and because a large share of TCS's revenue is billed in dollars and other hard currencies, every dollar of billing simply converted into more rupees. That lifts the reported top line without a single extra hour of work being sold. The second is the base: the June 2025 quarter was one of TCS's weakest in years, so the year-on-year comparison starts from a low floor. Strip the currency tailwind and the soft base away, and the constant-currency growth — the number that actually measures whether clients are buying more — was a small fraction of 14%. The company that "beat estimates" mostly beat them on the rupee.

2 · Where the 14% actually came from

The cleanest way to see the quarter is to separate what grew from what merely translated. Revenue climbed 14% in rupees; profit climbed roughly 5%. When your top line grows nearly three times faster than your bottom line, the growth is not falling through to the owners — it is being absorbed by costs, currency mechanics and one-off charges on the way down. That wedge is the single most important chart of the quarter.

A ₹668 crore exceptional charge explains part of the shortfall. It came from settling a long-running claim by Computer Sciences Corporation after the US Supreme Court declined to hear TCS's appeal; a further ₹69 crore was booked as interest to the date of settlement. That is a genuine one-off — profit excluding it looks healthier. But the charge is not the whole gap. Even before it, profit growth trailed revenue growth, because the costs that matter to a people business — salaries — rose faster than pricing could recover.

3 · The margin tells the truth

If revenue can be flattered by currency, the margin is harder to dress up. TCS reported an operating margin of 24.0% and a net margin of 19.2%. Both are respectable in absolute terms and would be the envy of most industries. But direction is what earnings are about, and the direction is down: 24.0% is below the 24.5% of a year ago and a clear step down from the 25.0% at which TCS closed FY26 — its best full-year margin in four years. The company gave back most of a year's margin progress in a single quarter.

Two forces pushed it down. TCS completed its annual salary increments for staff globally this quarter and re-aligned pay to India's new Labour Code — real, recurring cost that lands all at once. And AI-led work, the fast-growing part of the book, often carries different economics from the old time-and-materials model: clients increasingly pay for outcomes, not headcount-hours, which compresses the classic pricing lever. A margin at 24% is not a crisis. But a company whose growth is currency-assisted and whose margin is eroding is not, on this evidence, in a genuine up-cycle.

4 · The real story: AI, and fewer people

Underneath the soft translation is the strategic shift that actually matters. TCS put a number on its artificial-intelligence business for the first time in a way investors can track: annualised AI revenue of $2.6 billion, up 13.6% in a single quarter. That is still a slice of a roughly $34-billion-a-year run rate — but it is the only line growing at a genuinely fast clip, and it is compounding quarter on quarter. The order book backs it: the marquee win was an $800 million AI-led business-transformation deal with the Swedish engineering group SKF, alongside a strategic partnership with ServiceNow and a clutch of Fortune Global names.

Now put that next to the headcount. The workforce fell to 593,798 — down sharply from the 613,000-plus TCS carried a year earlier — while attrition stayed contained at 13.6%. A labour-arbitrage business is defined by one reflex: growth means hiring. When a services giant grows its reported revenue and shrinks its people at the same time, it is telling you the model is changing. The next dollar of margin is meant to come from software and AI leverage, not from putting another thousand engineers on a client's floor. That is the bet embedded in this quarter — and it is why the AI line, not the 14%, is the number to track.

5 · What actually moved the quarter

Stack the forces and the picture resolves. A few tailwinds — currency, a soft base, resilient deal wins, an accelerating AI line — pushed the headline up. The bottom line

TCS did what it always does: it set the tone for the season, and the tone is careful optimism dressed as growth. Fourteen percent is a good headline and a misleading one — most of it is the rupee and an easy base, not clients spending more. The honest signals sit lower in the release: a margin that slipped back to 24%, a profit that grew a third as fast as revenue, and a workforce that is shrinking. Against that, the one unambiguous bright spot is AI — a $2.6 billion annualised line growing double digits every quarter, backed by an $800 million transformation deal. That is where the company is placing its chips. So read this quarter for what it is: not the moment the cycle turned, but the moment you could see TCS betting that AI-led work replaces the labour-arbitrage engine before that engine fully stalls. The 14% will fade with the currency. Whether the $2.6 billion keeps compounding is the number that decides the next few years.
