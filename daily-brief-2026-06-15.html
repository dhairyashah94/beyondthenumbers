Hormuz Reopens, Oil Buckles, and Asia Roars Into the New Week

A US–Iran ceasefire confirmed over the weekend reopens the Strait of Hormuz and drains the war premium that has gripped crude since spring. Tokyo printed an all-time high, oil sank roughly 5%, and Wall Street futures point sharply higher — but the relief trade runs straight into a Federal Reserve that still has 4.2% inflation to answer for.

Five Things to Know This Morning

  1. The war is over. Washington and Tehran confirmed a ceasefire over the weekend; President Trump ordered the toll-free reopening of the Strait of Hormuz and lifted the US naval blockade, with a formal signing set for Switzerland on Friday.
  2. Oil's war premium is collapsing. Brent slid roughly 5% this morning toward the low-$80s after a 4%-plus drop Friday, unwinding most of the spike that drove crude above $100 during the conflict.
  3. Tokyo made history. The Nikkei 225 surged about 5.5% to clear 69,000 for the first time ever; Korea's Kospi jumped as much as 5.7% and US futures are pointing sharply higher.
  4. The Gulf's relief trade is on. Dubai's DFM — down roughly 16% during the war — and other regional bourses stand to reclaim losses as Hormuz reopens; Saudi Arabia's Tadawul held up far better, up around 2% through the conflict.
  5. The Fed still looms larger. With CPI running at a three-year-high 4.2% and the funds rate at 3.50–3.75%, Wednesday's FOMC decision and dot plot — not the ceasefire — is the week's real risk event.

The Big Picture

What changed over the weekend was not a headline but a structural risk. For three months the market has carried a Hormuz premium — a tax on every barrel, every freight rate and every Gulf equity to insure against the chance that a fifth of the world's seaborne oil simply stopped moving. The confirmed ceasefire, the lifted blockade and the order to reopen the strait remove that tail, and markets are repricing it with the speed they always reserve for relief rather than risk. Crude is reverting toward where supply-and-demand alone would put it; haven trades are unwinding in lockstep, with gold easing from above $4,200, the dollar softening and the VIX, already down 9% Friday to 17.7, set to open lower still. Tokyo's record and Seoul's near-6% leap are the cleanest expression of the logic: oil-importing Asia is the largest direct beneficiary of cheaper energy flowing freely through Hormuz again.

What the tape is not yet pricing is the distance between a deal and a functioning waterway. Iran's commitment is to reopen Hormuz within 30 days, and the strait must be cleared of mines, idled fields restarted and damaged facilities repaired before crude actually normalises — the agreement itself is not signed until Friday in Switzerland. So this is a re-rating of probability, not an all-clear: the market has marked down the odds of catastrophe, but the physical supply is still weeks from returning. That gap is the near-term risk, and it cuts both ways — any implementation stumble would snap the war premium straight back into the price.

Markets

Equities: Asia does the heavy lifting. Friday's modest Wall Street gains — the S&P 500 up 0.5% to 7,431.46, the Nasdaq Composite up 0.31% to 25,888.84 and the Dow up 0.7% to 51,202.26 — already look like a different era against this morning's surge. The Nikkei cleared 69,000 for the first time on a roughly 5.5% jump, Korea's Kospi leapt as much as 5.7% with semiconductors leading on strong export data, and last week's 19% Nasdaq debut for SpaceX is still adding to risk appetite. US futures are sharply higher ahead of the 5:30 PM GST reopen.

Rates & FX: the reopen pulls Treasuries two ways. The 10-year Treasury yield closed Friday at 4.49%, and the reopen tugs it in opposite directions: fading haven demand and a roaring risk bid argue for higher yields, but a 4.2% inflation print and a Fed two days from its decision cap how far Treasuries can sell off. The dollar index ended Friday at 99.81 and is softening this morning as the safe-haven trade unwinds — a classic risk-on tell that tends to flatter emerging-market and commodity-linked currencies.

Commodities & crypto: crude is the epicentre. Brent is down roughly 5% toward the low-$80s after Friday's slide below $86.50, retracing most of the conflict-era spike. Gold is easing from Friday's $4,210.91 as the war bid leaves the metal, while bitcoin has added 2.08% to $65,695, helped by a turn back to positive ETF inflows after weeks of redemptions and the broad return of risk appetite.

"The market is pricing the end of a war in a weekend; the strait will take a month to actually clear. That gap between the screen and the sea is where the next surprise lives."

The Relief Rally Walks Into the Fed

Cheaper oil is disinflationary at the margin, but it arrives too late to spare the Fed its hardest meeting of the year. Friday before last delivered the hottest CPI in three years at 4.2%, and with the funds rate sitting at 3.50–3.75% — below inflation — real policy is already negative, a setting that historically argues against the rate cuts markets spent the spring expecting. A hold on Wednesday is all but certain at a market-implied ~99.5% probability, so the action is in the Summary of Economic Projections. If the median dot pivots from easing toward a prolonged pause, or hints at a hike bias, the equity relief rally could meet a rates headwind just as it gets going.

The Bottom Line

The weekend removed a war; it did not remove the Fed. Markets are right to reprice a reopened Hormuz — that is real, and Asia's records prove it — but the easy part of the relief trade may be the part that finishes by lunchtime in Dubai. From here, two clocks matter: the 30 days it takes to physically clear the strait, and the 48 hours until Wednesday's dot plot tells investors whether 4.2% inflation has finally turned the Fed hawkish. Peace cut the tail risk. It did not cut the cost of money.
